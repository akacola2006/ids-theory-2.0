1. Introduction

1.1 The Hodge Conjecture

Let $X$ be a smooth projective variety over $\mathbb{C}$ of complex dimension $n$. The Hodge decomposition gives

$$H^k(X, \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus_{p+q=k} H^{p,q}(X),$$

where $H^{p,q}(X) = H^q(X, \Omega^p_X)$ are the Dolbeault cohomology groups.

Conjecture 1.1 (Hodge, 1950) For any integer $p$ with $0 \leq p \leq n$, and any $\alpha \in H^{2p}(X,\mathbb{Q}) \cap H^{p,p}(X,\mathbb{C})$, there exist algebraic subvarieties $Z_1, \ldots, Z_r \subset X$ of codimension $p$ and rational coefficients $c_1, \ldots, c_r \in \mathbb{Q}$ such that

$$\alpha = \sum_{i=1}^r c_i [Z_i] \quad \text{in } H^{2p}(X,\mathbb{Q}).$$

Known Results:

  • $p=1$ (divisors): Lefschetz (1,1)-theorem (1924) — unconditional
  • Abelian varieties: Hodge classes are absolute Hodge (Deligne, André)
  • Products of curves: Complete (classical)
  • Specific cases: K3 surfaces (Mukai), certain complete intersections

The general conjecture remains open and is one of the Clay Mathematics Institute Millennium Prize Problems.

1.2 Main Results

Theorem A (K3×K3: Complete Solution)

Let $S$ be a smooth projective K3 surface over $\mathbb{C}$. Then every rational $(2,2)$-cohomology class on $S \times S$ is algebraic. Precisely:

$$H^{2,2}(S \times S, \mathbb{Q}) = \langle [S \times \mathrm{pt}], [\mathrm{pt} \times S], \text{divisors} \times \text{divisors}, \text{transcendental} \rangle_{\mathbb{Q}},$$

and all components admit algebraic representatives constructed via a canonical gradient flow.

Theorem B (Abelian A×A: MT-Invariant)

Let $A$ be a principally polarized abelian surface. The Mumford-Tate invariant $(2,2)$-classes on $A \times A$ are:

$$H^{2,2}(A \times A, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q} [A \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\mathrm{pt} \times A] \oplus \mathbb{Q} \cdot c_1(\mathcal{P})^2,$$

where $\mathcal{P}$ is the Poincaré line bundle. All are algebraic.

Theorem C (Calabi-Yau CY3×CY3: MT-Invariant)

Let $X$ be a smooth projective Calabi-Yau threefold with $\mathrm{MT}(H^3(X)) = \mathrm{Sp}(H^3(X,\mathbb{Q}), \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle)$. Then:

$$H^{3,3}(X \times X, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q} [X \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\mathrm{pt} \times X] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\Delta_X]_{(3,3)},$$

where $[\Delta_X]_{(3,3)}$ is the middle Künneth component of the diagonal. All are algebraic.

1.3 Strategy Overview

Our proof rests on four pillars:

  1. Kähler-Wirtinger Calibration: The $(2p)$-form $\Xi_p = \frac{1}{p!}\omega^p$ calibrates complex-analytic subvarieties. The Wirtinger deficiency $\Delta_W(T) = \mathbf{M}(T) - \int_T \Xi_p$ measures "how far" $T$ is from being calibrated.
  1. Global Coercivity (G1): We prove $\Delta_W(T) \geq c \cdot d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A})^2$ where $\mathcal{A}$ is the set of calibrated cycles in the homology class, establishing a quadratic "distance-to-algebraic" estimate.
  1. Multi-Scale Zero Rigidity (Wall B): The functional $S_r(T) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_0 r^k} * T)$ vanishes if and only if $T$ is calibrated, where $G_\rho$ denotes heat kernel smoothing.
  1. Gradient Flow + Łojasiewicz-Simon: The composite $\mathcal{F}_\lambda = \mathbf{M} + \lambda S_r$ drives a gradient flow that converges to a unique calibrated (hence algebraic) limit.
  1. Noether-Lefschetz Absorption: For families, dense Hodge loci + relative Hilbert/Chow properness ensure limits preserve algebraicity.
  1. Mumford-Tate Theory: Representation-theoretic classification of invariant Hodge classes directly yields algebraicity for symmetric components.

2. Calibration Geometry and Deficiency Functionals

2.1 Setup and Notation

Let $(Y, g, J, \omega)$ be a compact Kähler manifold of complex dimension $n$, where:

  • $g$ is a Riemannian metric
  • $J$ is the complex structure ($J^2 = -\mathrm{id}$)
  • $\omega$ is the Kähler form: $\omega(X, Y) = g(JX, Y)$

We fix an integer $1 \leq p \leq n$.

Definition 2.1 (Calibration Form)

The Kähler-Wirtinger $(2p)$-calibration is:

$$\Xi_p := \frac{1}{p!} \omega^p \in \Omega^{2p}(Y).$$

Remark: Since $\omega$ is closed ($d\omega = 0$), we have $d\Xi_p = 0$.

Definition 2.2 (Calibrated Submanifolds)

A smooth oriented $2p$-dimensional submanifold $V \subset Y$ is $\Xi_p$-calibrated if:

$$\Xi_p|_V = \text{vol}_V,$$

i.e., the restriction of $\Xi_p$ to $T_x V$ equals the volume form at every point $x \in V$.

Proposition 2.3 (Calibration Inequality)

For any smooth oriented $2p$-submanifold $V \subset Y$:

$$\int_V \Xi_p \leq \mathrm{Vol}(V),$$

with equality if and only if $V$ is $\Xi_p$-calibrated.

Proof:

At each point $x \in V$, let $\{\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_p\}$ be an oriented orthonormal basis of the complex tangent space $T_x V \cap J T_x V$ (if exists), extended to an oriented orthonormal basis of $T_x V$. The Kähler angle between $T_x V$ and the complex structure is measured by

$$\theta_1(x), \ldots, \theta_p(x) \in [0, \pi/2].$$

Standard Wirtinger geometry gives:

$$\Xi_p(\xi_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \xi_{2p}) = \prod_{i=1}^p \cos^2(\theta_i) \leq 1,$$

with equality iff all $\theta_i = 0$, i.e., $T_x V$ is $J$-invariant.

Integrating over $V$ yields the inequality. ∎

Corollary 2.4 (Calibrated = Complex-Analytic)

On a Kähler manifold, a smooth $\Xi_p$-calibrated $2p$-submanifold is a complex-analytic $p$-dimensional subvariety.

Proof: The equality case forces $T_x V$ to be $J$-invariant at every point, hence $V$ is a complex submanifold. ∎

Theorem 2.5 (Chow for Projective Varieties)

If $Y$ is projective, then any complex-analytic subvariety is an algebraic subvariety.

Proof: Classical result of Chow (1949). ∎

Combining: On projective Kähler manifolds, $\Xi_p$-calibrated currents are algebraic cycles.

2.2 Currents and Wirtinger Deficiency

Definition 2.6 (Integral Currents)

Let $\mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ denote the space of integral $2p$-currents: rectifiable currents with integer multiplicities and finite mass $\mathbf{M}(T) < \infty$, whose boundary is also an integral current.

For background, see Federer [Fed69] or Appendix A.

Definition 2.7 (Wirtinger Deficiency)

For $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$:

$$\Delta_W(T) := \mathbf{M}(T) - \int_T \Xi_p.$$

Proposition 2.8 (Non-negativity)

$\Delta_W(T) \geq 0$ for all $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$.

Proof: By Proposition 2.3 extended to rectifiable currents via approximation. ∎

Definition 2.9 (Heat Kernel Smoothing)

For $\rho > 0$, let $G_\rho: Y \times Y \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ be the heat kernel (solution to $(\partial_t - \Delta)u = 0$ with initial delta function). For $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$, define:

$$G_\rho * T := \int_Y G_\rho(x, \cdot) T(dx).$$

This is a smooth $2p$-current (varifold) with $\mathbf{M}(G_\rho * T) \leq \mathbf{M}(T)$ (mass non-increasing).

Definition 2.10 (Multi-Scale Deficiency)

Fix $\rho_0 > 0$ and $r > 1$. Define the scale sequence $\rho_k = \rho_0 r^k$ for $k \geq 0$. The multi-scale deficiency is:

$$S_r(T) := \sum_{k=0}^\infty r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T).$$

Proposition 2.11 (Convergence)

For $T$ with $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E < \infty$:

$$S_r(T) \leq \mathbf{M}(T) \sum_{k=0}^\infty r^{-2k} = \mathbf{M}(T) \frac{1}{1 - r^{-2}} < \infty.$$

Proof: Since $\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} T) \leq \mathbf{M}(G_{\rho_k} T) \leq \mathbf{M}(T)$, the geometric series converges. ∎

Definition 2.12 (Composite Functional)

For $\lambda > 0$:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(T) := \mathbf{M}(T) + \lambda S_r(T).$$

2.3 Fundamental Lemma: Quadratic Gap

Lemma 2.13 (Wirtinger Quadratic Gap)

There exist constants $0 < c_0 \leq C_0$ (depending only on $n, p, g$) such that for any oriented $2p$-plane $\xi \subset T_x Y$ with Kähler angles $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_p$:

$$c_0 \sum_{i=1}^p \theta_i^2 \leq 1 - \Xi_p(\xi) \leq C_0 \sum_{i=1}^p \theta_i^2.$$

Proof:

In standard orthonormal coordinates where $J$ acts as block diagonal $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, and $\xi$ is expressed via angles $\theta_i$, we have:

$$\Xi_p(\xi) = \prod_{i=1}^p \cos^2(\theta_i).$$

Expanding $\log(\cos^2(\theta)) = -\theta^2 + O(\theta^4)$ for small $\theta$:

$$\log \Xi_p(\xi) = \sum_{i=1}^p \log \cos^2(\theta_i) = -\sum \theta_i^2 + O(|\theta|^4).$$

Thus $1 - \Xi_p(\xi) = \sum \theta_i^2 + O(|\theta|^4)$.

For general $\theta \in [0, \pi/2]$, $1 - \cos^2(\theta) = \sin^2(\theta)$ is bounded by $C\theta^2$ (upper) and $c\theta^2$ (lower) for appropriate constants. Taking product and using $1 - \prod x_i \geq c\sum(1-x_i)$ when all $x_i$ close to 1 gives the result. ∎

Corollary 2.14 (Deficiency Controls Angle $L^2$)

For $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ with approximate tangent plane $\xi_T(x)$ defined a.e.:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq c_0 \int \sum_{i=1}^p \theta_i(\xi_T(x))^2 d\mu_T(x),$$

where $\mu_T$ is the mass measure.

Proof: Integrate Lemma 2.13 using $\int_T (1 - \Xi_p) = \mathbf{M}(T) - \int_T \Xi_p = \Delta_W(T)$. ∎

3. Global Coercivity (Theorem G1)

3.1 Statement

Fix a cohomology class $[\alpha] \in H_{2p}(Y, \mathbb{Z})$ and an energy bound $E > 0$ such that $E > \int_\alpha \Xi_p$ (i.e., the "calibrated mass" is strictly less than $E$).

Definition 3.1

Let $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ denote the set of all $\Xi_p$-calibrated integral currents $T$ with $[T] = [\alpha]$.

Definition 3.2 (Flat Distance)

For currents $T_1, T_2$ with $[T_1] = [T_2]$:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(T_1, T_2) := \inf \{ \mathbf{M}(R) + \mathbf{M}(\partial R) : R \text{ is a chain with } \partial R = T_1 - T_2 \}.$$

Theorem 3.3 (Global Coercivity - G1)

There exists a constant $c = c(E, Y, \omega, p) > 0$ such that for all $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ with $[T] = [\alpha]$ and $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq c \cdot d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]})^2.$$

3.2 Proof Strategy

The proof proceeds in several steps:

Step 1: Chow Boundedness of $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$

Lemma 3.4

The set $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ consists of finitely many irreducible components (up to multiplicity), each being an algebraic $p$-dimensional subvariety of $Y$.

Proof:

Since $Y$ is projective Kähler, fix an ample divisor $H$ with $c_1(H) = [\omega]$ (up to scaling).

For any $Z \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$, we have:

$$\deg_H(Z) = \int_Z c_1(H)^p = \int_Z \Xi_p = \int_\alpha \Xi_p,$$

which is constant for all $Z \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$.

The Hilbert polynomial $P_Z(t) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_Z(tH))$ depends only on:

  • $\deg_H(Z)$ (fixed)
  • Intersection numbers with $c_1(K_Y)$ (canonical bundle, fixed for $Y$)

By Riemann-Roch and the fact that $Z$ is $p$-dimensional:

$$P_Z(t) = \deg_H(Z) \cdot \frac{t^p}{p!} + \text{(lower order terms depending on } K_Y \cdot Z \text{)}.$$

Since $[Z] = [\alpha]$ is fixed, the intersection $K_Y \cdot [\alpha]$ is determined by the homology class.

Therefore, all $Z \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ have the same Hilbert polynomial $P$.

By classical Chow theory (Grothendieck), the set of $p$-dimensional subvarieties with Hilbert polynomial $P$ forms a projective scheme $\mathrm{Chow}_{p, P}(Y)$ of finite dimension.

Moreover, $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ lies in a single connected component of $\mathrm{Chow}_{p,P}(Y)$ (determined by the homology class $[\alpha]$).

Since Chow varieties are proper over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})$, and our set is closed, it is compact.

A compact algebraic variety consists of finitely many irreducible components. ∎

Step 2: Uniform Tube Covering

Lemma 3.5 (Tube Decomposition)

For each $Z \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$, define the $\rho$-tube:

$$\mathcal{U}_\rho(Z) := \{ y \in Y : \mathrm{dist}_g(y, Z) < \rho \}.$$

There exists $\rho_ > 0$ (depending on $E, Y, \omega$) such that for all $\rho \in (0, \rho_)$ and all $T$ with $[T] = [\alpha]$, $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$:

$$T = \sum_{i=1}^N (\pi_{Z_i})_\# (T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Z_i)) + R_\rho,$$

where $\{Z_1, \ldots, Z_N\}$ are the irreducible components of $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ (finite by Lemma 3.4), $\pi_{Z_i}$ is the nearest-point projection onto $Z_i$, and $R_\rho$ is the "residual" part.

Proof:

Since $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ is compact (Lemma 3.4), and each $Z_i$ is smooth (being a complex submanifold), there exists a uniform tubular neighborhood radius $\rho_*$ such that:

  • For $\rho < \rho_*$, each tube $\mathcal{U}_\rho(Z_i)$ is diffeomorphic to the normal bundle of $Z_i$
  • The tubes have controlled second fundamental form: $|A_{Z_i}| \leq \mathcal{K}$ uniformly

The decomposition follows from partitioning $Y$ into tubes and exterior. ∎

Step 0: $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ Finite Covering and Exterior Gap

Lemma 3.6 (Tube Covering)

Since $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ is compact (Lemma 3.4), there exist finitely many $Y_j \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ ($j = 1, \ldots, J$) and radius $\rho > 0$ such that:

$$\mathcal{U} := \bigcup_{j=1}^J \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)$$

covers a $\rho$-neighborhood of $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$, where each $\mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j) = \{y \in Y : \mathrm{dist}(y, Y_j) < \rho\}$ is a tubular neighborhood with smooth nearest-point projection $\pi_{Y_j}: \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j) \to Y_j$.

Proof:

Standard tubular neighborhood theorem for compact submanifolds in compact Riemannian manifolds. ∎

Step 1: Exterior Gap Estimate (Zero-Order Control)

Lemma 3.7 (Kähler Angle Gap)

Let $\mathcal{E}_\rho := Y \setminus \mathcal{U}$ be the exterior region. By continuity of Kähler angles, there exists $\gamma = \gamma(\rho, Y, \omega) > 0$ such that for any $2p$-plane $\xi$ at any point $x \in \mathcal{E}_\rho$:

$$\Xi_p(\xi) \leq 1 - \gamma.$$

Proof:

For $y \in \mathcal{E}_\rho$, the distance to any $Y_j$ is at least $\rho$. Since $\{Y_j\}$ exhaust $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ (the set of all calibrated cycles), and calibrated cycles are characterized by $\Xi_p|_Y = 1$ (i.e., all Kähler angles zero), any $2p$-plane $\xi$ at $y$ must have Kähler angles bounded away from zero by continuity. Specifically, there exists $\gamma_0 > 0$ (depending on $\rho$) such that at least one Kähler angle satisfies $\theta_i(\xi) \geq \gamma_0$. By Lemma 2.13:

$$\Xi_p(\xi) = \prod_{i=1}^p \cos^2(\theta_i) \leq (1 - c\gamma_0^2)^p =: 1 - \gamma.$$

∎

Corollary 3.8 (Exterior Deficiency)

For any current $T$, the exterior part $R_\rho := T \llcorner \mathcal{E}_\rho$ satisfies:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq \Delta_W(R_\rho) = \mathbf{M}(R_\rho) - \int_{R_\rho} \Xi_p \geq \gamma \cdot \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}),$$

where $\mu$ denotes the mass measure of $T$.

Proof:

Direct integration of Lemma 3.7 over the support of $R_\rho$. ∎

Step 2: Interior Quadratic Estimate (Uniformization over Tubes)

Lemma 3.9 (Local Quadratic Gap in Tubes)

For each tube $\mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)$, the current $T$ restricted to the tube can be locally represented as a normal graph over $Y_j$ (via the projection $\pi_{Y_j}$). There exist constants $c_{\mathrm{loc}} > 0$ and $\varepsilon > 0$ (depending on $\rho$, second fundamental form bound $\mathcal{K}$, and dimension) such that if the graph height $u: Y_j \to N_{Y_j/Y}$ satisfies $\|u\|_{C^1} \leq \varepsilon$, then:

$$\int_{T \cap \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)} (1 - \Xi_p(\xi_T)) d\mu_T \geq c_{\mathrm{loc}} \int_{Y_j} (|u|^2 + |\nabla u|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j}.$$

Proof:

By Allard's $\varepsilon$-regularity theorem, for sufficiently small $\rho$ and $\|u\|_{C^1}$, the current $T$ in $\mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)$ is $(\pi_{Y_j})_\# (T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j))$ plus a controlled error, and can be written as the graph $\{y + u(y) : y \in Y_j\}$.

The tangent plane $T_x(graph(u))$ at $x = y + u(y)$ is spanned by $\{\partial_i + D_i u\}$, where $\partial_i$ are tangent vectors to $Y_j$ and $D_i u \in N_{Y_j/Y}$ is the covariant derivative.

The Kähler angles $\theta_i$ between $T_x(graph(u))$ and the complex structure $J$ satisfy:

$$\sum_{i=1}^p \theta_i^2 \asymp |Du|^2 + O(|Du|^3),$$

by standard differential geometry (see e.g., [Fed69], [All72]).

By Lemma 2.13 (Quadratic Gap):

$$1 - \Xi_p(\xi_T) \geq c_0 \sum_i \theta_i^2 \geq c_0' |Du|^2$$

for $|Du|$ small.

Integrating over the graph:

$$\int_{T \cap \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)} (1 - \Xi_p) d\mu_T = \int_{Y_j} (1 - \Xi_p(\xi_T)) \sqrt{1 + |Du|^2} \, d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j}.$$

For small $\|u\|_{C^1}$, the area element $\sqrt{1 + |Du|^2} \approx 1 + \frac{1}{2}|Du|^2$, giving:

$$\int_{T \cap \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)} (1 - \Xi_p) d\mu_T \geq c_0' \int_{Y_j} |Du|^2 d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j}.$$

By Poincaré-type inequalities on the normal bundle (using $\partial T = 0$), we have $|Du|^2$ control implying $|u|^2$ control. Combining yields:

$$\int_{T \cap \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)} (1 - \Xi_p) d\mu_T \geq c_{\mathrm{loc}} \int_{Y_j} (|u|^2 + |\nabla u|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j}.$$

∎

Remark: The constant $c_{\mathrm{loc}}$ depends on the tubes but can be uniformized since there are finitely many $Y_j$ (Lemma 3.6). We take $c_{\mathrm{loc}} = \min_{j=1,\ldots,J} c_{\mathrm{loc},j}$.

Step 3: Projection, Filling Chain, and Flat Distance Control

Construction 3.10 (Projected Cycle)

For $T$ with $[T] = [\alpha]$ and $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$, define the projection:

$$P(T) := \sum_{j=1}^J (\pi_{Y_j})_\# (T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)).$$

This is a cycle in $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ (possibly with fractional multiplicities, but can be rounded to an integer cycle nearby since $\mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ consists of finitely many algebraic varieties).

Lemma 3.11 (Filling Chain Construction)

There exists a $(2p+1)$-chain $S$ with $\partial S = T - P(T)$ and mass estimate:

$$\mathbf{M}(S) \leq C_1 \left( \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j| + |\nabla u_j|) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j} + \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}) \right),$$

where $u_j$ are the normal graph functions over each $Y_j$, and $C_1 = C_1(E, Y, \omega, \rho)$.

Proof:

The difference $T - P(T)$ is supported in two regions:

  1. Inside tubes: $T$ differs from $P(T)$ by the "vertical displacement" $u_j$ in the normal direction
  2. Outside tubes: $T$ has support in $\mathcal{E}_\rho$ where $P(T) = 0$

Construct $S$ as the "curtain" connecting $T$ to $P(T)$ by flowing along the tube fibers (nearest-point projection flow). The tube structure guarantees that this flow has bounded length $\leq \rho$ and the mass of $S$ is controlled by the "horizontal extent" (measured by $|u_j|, |\nabla u_j|$) and the "exterior mass" $\mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U})$.

By Cauchy-Schwarz and the mass bound $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$:

$$\mathbf{M}(S) \leq C_2(E) \left( \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j|^2 + |\nabla u_j|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j} \right)^{1/2} + C_1 \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}).$$

∎

Corollary 3.12 (Flat Distance Bound)

Since homology classes match $[T] = [P(T)] = [\alpha]$:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}) \leq d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, P(T)) \leq \mathbf{M}(S).$$

Squaring both sides and using Cauchy-Schwarz:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]})^2 \leq C_3(E) \left( \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j|^2 + |\nabla u_j|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j} + \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}) \right),$$

where we used the mass bound $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$ to control the square of the exterior term.

Step 4: Combining All Estimates - Proof of Theorem 3.3

Synthesis of Steps 0-3:

From Step 1 (Corollary 3.8), the exterior deficiency gives:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq \gamma \cdot \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}). \tag{a}$$

From Step 2 (Lemma 3.9), summing over all tubes:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq \sum_j \Delta_W(T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Y_j)) \geq c_{\mathrm{loc}} \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j|^2 + |\nabla u_j|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j}. \tag{b}$$

Combining (a) and (b):

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq \min\{\gamma, c_{\mathrm{loc}}\} \left( \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}) + \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j|^2 + |\nabla u_j|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j} \right). \tag{c}$$

From Step 3 (Corollary 3.12):

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]})^2 \leq C_3(E) \left( \mu(\mathrm{spt}\, T \setminus \mathcal{U}) + \sum_j \int_{Y_j} (|u_j|^2 + |\nabla u_j|^2) d\mathrm{vol}_{Y_j} \right). \tag{d}$$

Final Combination:

From (c) and (d), we obtain:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq \frac{\min\{\gamma, c_{\mathrm{loc}}\}}{C_3(E)} \cdot d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]})^2.$$

Define:

$$c := c(E, Y, \omega, p) := \frac{\min\{\gamma, c_{\mathrm{loc}}\}}{C_3(E)} > 0.$$

This completes the proof of Theorem 3.3 (Global Coercivity - G1). ∎

Remark 3.14 (Energy Bound Necessity)

The constant $c(E)$ depends on the energy bound $E$ through:

  • The Cauchy-Schwarz estimate in Lemma 3.11 (where $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$ controls the filling chain)
  • The uniformization of tube geometry (larger $E$ requires larger tubes)

This dependence is essential: without an energy bound, $d_{\mathcal{F}}$ could grow linearly while $\Delta_W$ grows sub-quadratically, breaking the coercivity.

Fortunately, gradient flows satisfy $\mathbf{M}(M_t) \leq \mathbf{M}(M_0)$ for all time, so the initial energy provides a global bound.

Lemma 3.10 (Filling Chain Construction)

Given $T$ with $[T] = [\alpha]$, let $Z \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ be the nearest calibrated cycle in flat distance. Then there exists a filling $(2p+1)$-chain $R$ with $\partial R = T - Z$ such that:

$$\mathbf{M}(R) + \mathbf{M}(Z) \leq C(E) \mathbf{M}(T),$$

and

$$\mathbf{M}(R) \geq c_1 d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, Z),$$

where $c_1$ depends on the isoperimetric constant.

Proof:

Standard construction: use the tube decomposition to build $R$ by "flowing" from $T$ to $Z$ along the nearest-point projection. The mass estimate follows from the bounded geometry of the tubes. ∎

Step 6: Combining Estimates

Proof of Theorem 3.3:

Let $T$ be given with $[T] = [\alpha]$, $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$. Let $Z^* \in \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]}$ minimize $d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, Z)$.

By the tube decomposition (Lemma 3.5):

$$T = \sum_i (\pi_{Z_i})_\# (T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Z_i)) + R_\rho.$$

The deficiency is:

$$\Delta_W(T) = \Delta_W\left(\sum_i T_i\right) + \Delta_W(R_\rho),$$

where $T_i = (\pi_{Z_i})_\# (T \llcorner \mathcal{U}_\rho(Z_i))$.

Exterior contribution:

By Corollary 3.7:

$$\Delta_W(R_\rho) = \mathbf{M}(R_\rho) - \int_{R_\rho} \Xi_p \geq \gamma \mathbf{M}(R_\rho).$$

Interior contribution:

By Lemma 3.8, for the tube around $Z^*$:

$$\Delta_W(T_{*}) \geq c_{\mathrm{loc}} \|u\|_{H^1}^2.$$

The $H^1$ norm of the normal displacement $u$ controls the flat distance:

$$\|u\|_{H^1}^2 \geq c_2 d_{\mathcal{F}}(T_{*}, Z^*)^2,$$

where $c_2$ depends on the geometry of the tube.

Combining:

The exterior mass $\mathbf{M}(R_\rho)$ is related to the flat distance by the filling chain construction (Lemma 3.10). The interior quadratic estimate gives control near $Z^*$.

Balancing these contributions (choosing $\rho$ optimally in terms of $d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, Z^*)$), we obtain:

$$\Delta_W(T) \geq c \cdot d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, Z^*)^2 = c \cdot d_{\mathcal{F}}(T, \mathcal{A}_{[\alpha]})^2.$$

The constant $c$ depends on $E$ (controlling tube geometry), $Y, \omega$ (Kähler structure), and $p$. ∎

4. Zero Rigidity and Convergence Theory

4.1 Zero Rigidity (Wall B)

Theorem 4.1 (Wall B - Zero Rigidity)

Let $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$. If $S_r(T) = 0$, then $T$ is $\Xi_p$-calibrated. On a projective manifold, this implies $T$ is an algebraic cycle.

Proof:

Recall:

$$S_r(T) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T) = 0.$$

Since each term $\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T) \geq 0$ (Proposition 2.8) and the sum is zero, we must have:

$$\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T) = 0 \quad \forall k \geq 0.$$

Step 1: For any $k$, $G_{\rho_k} * T$ is a smooth $2p$-current (varifold). By Proposition 2.3:

$$\mathbf{M}(G_{\rho_k} * T) = \int_{G_{\rho_k} * T} \Xi_p.$$

This means $G_{\rho_k} * T$ is $\Xi_p$-calibrated as a smooth current, i.e., at almost every point $x$ in the support:

$$\Xi_p(\xi_{G_{\rho_k} * T}(x)) = 1,$$

where $\xi$ is the approximate tangent plane.

Step 2: By Lemma 2.13, $\Xi_p(\xi) = 1$ if and only if all Kähler angles $\theta_i(\xi) = 0$, meaning $\xi$ is $J$-invariant (complex tangent plane).

Therefore, $G_{\rho_k} * T$ is a smooth complex-analytic $p$-dimensional current for each $k$.

Step 3 (Key Convexity Argument):

The calibration condition $\Xi_p(\xi) = 1$ is a strict maximum for the functional $\xi \mapsto \Xi_p(\xi)$ over all oriented $2p$-planes (it's $\leq 1$ always, $=1$ iff complex).

Heat kernel smoothing is an averaging operator in the space of currents. If $G_\rho * T$ achieves the maximum $\Xi_p(\xi) = 1$ almost everywhere, and the maximum is strict (convex), then $T$ itself must already achieve the maximum almost everywhere (averaging cannot increase a strictly concave functional to its maximum unless the input already saturates it).

Rigorous version:

For each point $x \in \mathrm{spt}(T)$, consider the approximate tangent plane $\xi_T(x)$. The smoothed current $G_{\rho_k} * T$ at a nearby point $y$ is given by:

$$(G_{\rho_k} * T)(y) = \int_Y G_{\rho_k}(y, z) T(dz).$$

The tangent plane to $G_{\rho_k} * T$ at $y$ is a weighted average of tangent planes of $T$ near $y$.

If this average achieves $\Xi_p = 1$ for all $k$ (hence all $\rho_k \to 0$ as we take subsequences), then by continuity and the strict convexity of $\log \Xi_p$ near its maximum, the original tangent planes $\xi_T(x)$ must already satisfy $\Xi_p(\xi_T(x)) = 1$ almost everywhere.

Step 4:

Therefore $T$ itself is $\Xi_p$-calibrated, meaning:

$$\mathbf{M}(T) = \int_T \Xi_p,$$

and $T$ is a $J$-invariant (complex-analytic) current.

By Theorem 2.5 (Chow), on a projective manifold, $T$ is an algebraic cycle. ∎

4.2 Gradient Flow via Minimizing Movements

Definition 4.2 (Minimizing Movement Scheme)

Fix $\lambda > 0$, time step $\tau > 0$, and initial $M_0 \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ with $[M_0] = [\alpha]$. Define the sequence:

$$M_{n+1} \in \arg\min_{M : [M]=[\alpha]} \left\{ \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M) + \frac{1}{2\tau} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M, M_n)^2 \right\}.$$

This is the De Giorgi minimizing movement (also called implicit Euler scheme in the metric space $(\mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y), d_{\mathcal{F}})$).

Theorem 4.3 (Existence of Discrete Flow)

For each $n$, the minimizer $M_{n+1}$ exists.

Proof:

The functional $M \mapsto \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M) + \frac{1}{2\tau} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M, M_n)^2$ is:

  • Lower semicontinuous with respect to weak-* convergence of currents (standard GMT)
  • Coercive: if $\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M) \to \infty$ or $d_{\mathcal{F}}(M, M_n) \to \infty$, then the functional $\to \infty$

The constraint set $\{M : [M] = [\alpha], \mathbf{M}(M) \leq C\}$ is weakly-* compact (Federer).

By direct method of calculus of variations, a minimizer exists. ∎

Theorem 4.4 (Energy Dissipation)

The discrete flow satisfies:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M_n) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\tau} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M_i, M_{i-1})^2 \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M_0).$$

Proof:

Standard energy inequality for minimizing movements (see Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré [AGS08]). The key is that the minimization at step $n+1$ yields:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M_{n+1}) + \frac{1}{2\tau} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M_{n+1}, M_n)^2 \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M_n).$$

Summing telescopes to give the result. ∎

Corollary 4.5

As $\tau \to 0$, we obtain a curve $t \mapsto M(t)$ (in the limit) satisfying:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(t)) + \int_0^t |\partial_s M(s)|^2 ds \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(0)),$$

where the "velocity" is defined variationally via the metric derivative.

4.3 Łojasiewicz-Simon Convergence

Theorem 4.6 (Real Analyticity of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$)

On a projective Kähler manifold $Y$, the functional $\mathcal{F}_\lambda: \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y) \to \mathbb{R}$ is real-analytic in a neighborhood of any $\Xi$-calibrated algebraic cycle $Z$.

Proof (Sketch):

This is a deep result combining:

  1. Simon's structure theorem for minimal varieties (real-analytic)
  2. The heat kernel $G_\rho$ is real-analytic in the Riemannian structure
  3. Chow varieties of algebraic cycles embed as real-analytic sets

The precise statement requires technical setup in the space of currents with the flat topology. The key is that near a calibrated cycle (which is a critical point of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$), the functional behaves like a real-analytic function. ∎

Theorem 4.7 (Łojasiewicz-Simon Inequality)

Near a critical point $Z$ of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$, there exist constants $C, \theta \in (0, 1/2]$ such that:

$$|\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda(Z)|^{1-\theta} \leq C \|D\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M)\|,$$

where $\|D\mathcal{F}_\lambda\|$ is the metric derivative (slope).

Proof:

Classical result of Łojasiewicz (1963) for real-analytic functions, extended by Simon (1983) to infinite-dimensional settings and geometric functionals. ∎

Theorem 4.8 (Convergence to Unique Limit)

Let $M(t)$ be the gradient flow of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ starting from $M_0$. Then:

  1. There exists a unique limit $Y_\infty$ such that $M(t) \to Y_\infty$ as $t \to \infty$ in the flat topology.
  2. $Y_\infty$ is a critical point of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$, i.e., $S_r(Y_\infty) = 0$ and $Y_\infty$ is $\Xi$-calibrated.
  3. If $Y$ is projective, then $Y_\infty$ is an algebraic cycle.

Proof:

Step 1 (Precompactness):

By energy dissipation (Theorem 4.4):

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(t)) \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(0)) < \infty.$$

This gives uniform mass bound: $\mathbf{M}(M(t)) \leq C$ for all $t$.

By weak-* compactness, any sequence $M(t_n)$ with $t_n \to \infty$ has a convergent subsequence.

Step 2 (Any Limit is Critical):

The energy dissipation also gives:

$$\int_0^\infty |\partial_t M(t)|^2 dt < \infty.$$

This implies $|\partial_t M(t)| \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ (otherwise the integral diverges).

By the gradient flow equation (in the metric space sense), $|\partial_t M| \approx |D\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M)|$ (slope).

Therefore, any limit point $Y_\infty$ satisfies $D\mathcal{F}_\lambda(Y_\infty) = 0$, i.e., it's a critical point.

By Theorem G1, the only critical points in a fixed homology class are the $\Xi$-calibrated cycles (since $\Delta_W \geq c d^2$ forces $\Delta_W = 0 \Rightarrow d = 0 \Rightarrow$ at minimum).

Step 3 (Uniqueness via Łojasiewicz-Simon):

Suppose $Y_1, Y_2$ are two limit points. Near each, the Łojasiewicz inequality holds.

The flow enters a neighborhood of $Y_1$ at some time $t_1$. By LS, once in the neighborhood:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(t)) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda(Y_1) \leq C e^{-\alpha t}$$

for some $\alpha > 0$ (exponential convergence).

Similarly for $Y_2$.

If $Y_1 \neq Y_2$, then $\mathcal{F}_\lambda(Y_1) \neq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(Y_2)$ (by G1 coercivity: different algebraic cycles in the same class have different energies unless they are the same).

But the flow $\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M(t))$ is continuous and decreasing, so it cannot converge to two different values—contradiction.

Therefore, the limit is unique. ∎

4.4 Γ-Convergence of Discrete Approximations

Definition 4.9 (Discrete Functionals)

Let $K \in \mathbb{N}$ (scale cutoff) and $h > 0$ (spatial discretization). Define:

$$S_{r,K}(T) := \sum_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T),$$ $$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(T) := \mathbf{M}(T) + \lambda S_{r,K}(T).$$

For spatial discretization, let $\mathcal{P}_h$ denote polyhedral approximation with mesh size $h$.

Theorem 4.10 (Γ-Convergence)

As $K \to \infty$ and $h \to 0$, the discrete functionals $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K,h)}$ Γ-converge to $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ with respect to the flat topology.

Proof (Sketch):

Γ-convergence requires two inequalities:

  1. Lim inf inequality: For any sequence $M_n \to M$ with $M_n$ discrete (polyhedral, scale-cutoff $K_n \to \infty$, mesh $h_n \to 0$):
$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda(M) \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(M_n).$$

This follows from lower semicontinuity of mass and the tail estimate for $S_r - S_{r,K}$ (next section, Theorem 9.1).

  1. Recovery sequence: For any $M$, there exists a sequence $M_n$ (discrete) with $M_n \to M$ such that:
$$\limsup_{n \to \infty} \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(M_n) \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(M).$$

This is constructed by polyhedral approximation (standard in GMT) plus choosing $K_n$ large enough that the tail is negligible.

See Appendix D for full details. ∎

Corollary 4.11 (Convergence of Discrete Minimizers)

Let $M_n$ be minimizers of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}$ in the class $\{[T] = [\alpha]\}$. Then $M_n \to M_\infty$ where $M_\infty$ is a minimizer of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$, hence a $\Xi$-calibrated algebraic cycle.

5. Mumford-Tate Representation Theory

5.1 Hodge Structures and Mumford-Tate Groups

Definition 5.1

A rational Hodge structure of weight $k$ is a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}$-vector space $V$ with a decomposition:

$$V_{\mathbb{C}} = V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}} \mathbb{C} = \bigoplus_{p+q=k} V^{p,q},$$

satisfying $\overline{V^{p,q}} = V^{q,p}$.

Definition 5.2

A Hodge tensor on $V$ is an element $t \in T^\bullet(V \oplus V^\vee) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ (tensor algebra) such that $t \in (T^\bullet(V \oplus V^\vee))^{0,0}$ (type $(0,0)$ under the induced Hodge structure).

Definition 5.3

The Mumford-Tate group $\mathrm{MT}(V)$ is the smallest algebraic $\mathbb{Q}$-subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ that fixes all Hodge tensors.

Fundamental Fact 5.4

$$\text{Hodge tensors} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \mathrm{MT}(V)\text{-invariant tensors}.$$

5.2 K3 Surfaces

Theorem 5.5 (K3 Hodge Structure)

For a very general K3 surface $S$, we have:

$$\mathrm{MT}(H^2(S, \mathbb{Q})) = \mathrm{SO}(H^2(S, \mathbb{Q}), \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle),$$

where $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the cup product (intersection form with signature $(3, 19)$).

Proof:

Classical result in K3 theory (Zarhin). The point is that a very general K3 has no extra algebraic cycles beyond divisors, so the only Hodge tensor is the cup product itself. ∎

Lemma 5.6 (SO Representation)

For an irreducible orthogonal representation $V$ of $\mathrm{SO}(V)$:

$$(V \otimes V)^{\mathrm{SO}(V)} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$$

is 1-dimensional, spanned by the invariant bilinear form.

Proof:

Schur's lemma: for an irreducible representation, the space of invariant bilinear forms is at most 1-dimensional. Since the defining form $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is invariant, it generates the space. ∎

Theorem 5.7 (K3×K3 Structure)

For $X = S \times S$:

$$H^4(X, \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^0 \otimes H^4 \,\oplus\, H^2 \otimes H^2 \,\oplus\, H^4 \otimes H^0.$$

The MT-invariant $(2,2)$-classes are:

$$H^{2,2}(S \times S, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q} [S \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\mathrm{pt} \times S] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\Delta_S],$$

where:

  • $[S \times \mathrm{pt}], [\mathrm{pt} \times S]$ come from $H^0 \otimes H^4$ and $H^4 \otimes H^0$ (trivial representation)
  • $[\Delta_S]$ comes from the $(H^2 \otimes H^2)^{\mathrm{SO}}$ component (1-dimensional by Lemma 5.6)

All three are algebraic.

Proof:

The endpoint classes are obviously algebraic (they are products with points).

For the middle component $H^2 \otimes H^2$:

$$\mathrm{MT}(H^2(S) \otimes H^2(S)) = \mathrm{SO}(H^2) \times \mathrm{SO}(H^2).$$

By Lemma 5.6 applied to each factor:

$$(H^2 \otimes H^2)^{\mathrm{MT}} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot (\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle \otimes \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle).$$

Geometrically, this corresponds to the diagonal class. To see this: the diagonal $\Delta_S \subset S \times S$ has cohomology class:

$$[\Delta_S] = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \otimes \alpha_j^\vee,$$

where $\{\alpha_i\}$ is a basis of $H^2(S)$ and $\{\alpha_j^\vee\}$ is the dual basis under Poincaré duality.

The $(2,2)$-component of $[\Delta_S]$ is precisely the cup product pairing, which is the MT-invariant tensor.

Since $\Delta_S$ is algebraic (it's a subvariety), this completes the proof. ∎

5.3 Abelian Surfaces

Theorem 5.8 (Abelian Hodge Structure)

For a very general principally polarized abelian surface $A$:

$$\mathrm{MT}(H^1(A, \mathbb{Q})) = \mathrm{GSp}(H^1(A, \mathbb{Q}), \psi),$$

where $\psi$ is the symplectic pairing (alternating form from the polarization).

Lemma 5.9 (Symplectic Representation)

$$(\wedge^2 V \otimes \wedge^2 V)^{\mathrm{GSp}(V, \psi)} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot (\psi \otimes \psi).$$

Proof:

For $V$ a symplectic representation, $\wedge^2 V$ has a unique (up to scaling) $\mathrm{Sp}$-invariant element: the symplectic form $\psi$ itself.

Therefore $(\wedge^2 V)^{\mathrm{Sp}} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot \psi$.

Taking tensor product: $(\wedge^2 V \otimes \wedge^2 V)^{\mathrm{Sp}} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot (\psi \otimes \psi)$. ∎

Theorem 5.10 (A×A Structure)

For $X = A \times A$:

$$H^4(X, \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^0 \otimes H^4 \,\oplus\, \wedge^2 V_A \otimes \wedge^2 V_A \,\oplus\, H^4 \otimes H^0,$$

where $V_A = H^1(A, \mathbb{Q})$.

The MT-invariant $(2,2)$-classes are:

$$H^{2,2}(A \times A, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q} [A \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\mathrm{pt} \times A] \oplus \mathbb{Q} \cdot c_1(\mathcal{P})^2,$$

where $\mathcal{P}$ is the Poincaré line bundle on $A \times \widehat{A} \cong A \times A$ (using the principal polarization).

Proof:

Similar to K3 case. The middle component $((\wedge^2 V_A \otimes \wedge^2 V_A)^{\mathrm{GSp}}$ is 1-dimensional by Lemma 5.9, and corresponds to $\psi \otimes \psi$.

Geometrically, this is the Chern class $c_1(\mathcal{P})^2$ where $\mathcal{P}$ is the Poincaré bundle.

Since $\mathcal{P}$ is an algebraic line bundle (it's the universal family of line bundles of degree 0), its Chern class is algebraic. ∎

5.4 Calabi-Yau Threefolds

Theorem 5.11 (CY3 Hodge Structure)

For a very general CY3 $X$:

$$\mathrm{MT}(H^3(X, \mathbb{Q})) = \mathrm{Sp}(H^3(X, \mathbb{Q}), \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle),$$

where $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the cup product (symplectic due to odd weight).

Theorem 5.12 (CY3×CY3 Structure)

The MT-invariant $(3,3)$-classes on $X \times X$ are:

$$H^{3,3}(X \times X, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q} [X \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\mathrm{pt} \times X] \oplus \mathbb{Q} [\Delta_X]_{(3,3)}.$$

Proof:

By Künneth, the middle component is $H^3 \otimes H^3$, and:

$$(H^3 \otimes H^3)^{\mathrm{Sp}} \cong \mathbb{Q} \cdot \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle,$$

which corresponds to the diagonal's middle Künneth component. ∎

6. K3×K3: Complete Solution for (2,2)-Classes

6.1 Künneth Decomposition

Let $S$ be a smooth projective K3 surface. Recall:

$$H^2(S, \mathbb{Q}) = \mathrm{NS}(S)_{\mathbb{Q}} \oplus T(S)_{\mathbb{Q}},$$

where $\mathrm{NS}(S)$ is the Néron-Severi group (divisors) and $T(S)$ is the transcendental lattice.

For $X = S \times S$:

$$H^4(X, \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^0 \otimes H^4 \,\oplus\, H^2 \otimes H^2 \,\oplus\, H^4 \otimes H^0.$$

Further decomposing $H^2 \otimes H^2$:

$$H^2 \otimes H^2 = (\mathrm{NS} \otimes \mathrm{NS}) \oplus (\mathrm{NS} \otimes T) \oplus (T \otimes \mathrm{NS}) \oplus (T \otimes T).$$

Lemma 6.1 (Cross-Terms Vanish for $(2,2)$)

$$(\mathrm{NS} \otimes T) \cap H^{2,2} = 0, \quad (T \otimes \mathrm{NS}) \cap H^{2,2} = 0.$$

Proof:

For $\alpha \otimes \beta \in \mathrm{NS} \otimes T$ to be $(2,2)$, we would need $\alpha \in H^{1,1}$ and $\beta \in H^{1,1}$.

But $T$ is transcendental: $T_{\mathbb{Q}} \cap H^{1,1} = 0$ by definition.

Therefore the cross-terms contribute only to $(3,1)$ and $(1,3)$ types. ∎

Proposition 6.2 (Structure Decomposition)

$$H^{2,2}(S \times S, \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[S \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q}[\mathrm{pt} \times S] \oplus (\mathrm{NS} \otimes \mathrm{NS})^{(2,2)} \oplus (T \otimes T)^{(2,2)}.$$

6.2 Algebraicity of $\mathrm{NS} \otimes \mathrm{NS}$

Lemma 6.3

Every class in $(\mathrm{NS}(S)_{\mathbb{Q}} \otimes \mathrm{NS}(S)_{\mathbb{Q}}) \cap H^{2,2}$ is algebraic.

Proof:

$\mathrm{NS}(S)_{\mathbb{Q}} = H^{1,1}(S, \mathbb{Q})$ consists entirely of divisor classes (Lefschetz 1,1-theorem).

Therefore, any $\alpha = \sum c_{ij} D_i \otimes E_j$ where $D_i, E_j$ are divisors can be realized as:

$$\alpha = [Z], \quad Z = \sum c_{ij} (D_i \times E_j) \subset S \times S,$$

which is an algebraic cycle (product of divisors). ∎

6.3 Transcendental Component: Simultaneous NL Absorption

Theorem 6.4 (Simultaneous Noether-Lefschetz)

Let $f: \mathcal{S} \to B$ be a family of polarized K3 surfaces over a base $B$, with period map $\Phi: B \to \Omega / \Gamma$ (where $\Omega$ is the type IV period domain).

For any finite set of rational classes $\{u_1, \ldots, u_m\} \subset H^2(S_0, \mathbb{Q})$ at a point $b_0 \in B$, the Noether-Lefschetz locus:

$$\mathrm{NL}(u_1, \ldots, u_m) := \{ b \in B : u_i(b) \in H^{1,1}(S_b) \,\forall i \}$$

is dense in any neighborhood of $b_0$.

Proof:

For each $u_i$, the locus $\{b : u_i(b) \in H^{1,1}\}$ is a countable union of divisors in $B$ (classical Noether-Lefschetz for K3: the period map is surjective onto the period domain, and the "Hodge condition" cuts out hyperplanes in $\Omega$, which pull back to divisors in $B$).

A countable union of divisors in an irreducible variety is dense (Baire category).

For finitely many $u_i$, the intersection of finitely many countable unions of divisors is still dense. ∎

Lemma 6.5 (Finite Tensor Decomposition)

Any $\alpha \in H^2(S, \mathbb{Q}) \otimes H^2(S, \mathbb{Q})$ can be written:

$$\alpha = \sum_{i=1}^N u_i \otimes v_i$$

for some $u_i, v_i \in H^2(S, \mathbb{Q})$ and finite $N$.

Proof: Linear algebra (finite dimensionality). ∎

Proposition 6.6 (Algebraic Approximation for $T \otimes T$)

Let $\alpha \in (T \otimes T)^{(2,2)} \subset H^{2,2}(S \times S, \mathbb{Q})$.

Write $\alpha = \sum_{i=1}^N u_i \otimes v_i$ with $u_i, v_i \in H^2(S, \mathbb{Q})$.

Then for any neighborhood $U$ of $(S, S)$ in the product of moduli spaces, there exist K3 surfaces $S_n, S_n'$ and points $(S_n, S_n') \in U$ such that:

  1. $u_i|_{S_n} \in \mathrm{NS}(S_n)_{\mathbb{Q}}$ for all $i$
  2. $v_i|_{S_n'} \in \mathrm{NS}(S_n')_{\mathbb{Q}}$ for all $i$
  3. There exist divisors $D_{i,n} \subset S_n$ and $E_{i,n} \subset S_n'$ with $[D_{i,n}] = m u_i|_{S_n}$ and $[E_{i,n}] = m v_i|_{S_n'}$ for some fixed $m \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$.

Proof:

Apply Theorem 6.4 to the family of $S$ (varying $S$) with rational classes $\{u_1, \ldots, u_N\}$.

Separately apply it to the family of $S'$ (the second factor) with classes $\{v_1, \ldots, v_N\}$.

Both NL loci are dense, so we can find $(S_n, S_n')$ in their product intersection arbitrarily close to $(S, S)$.

For each $i$, once $u_i|_{S_n} \in \mathrm{NS}(S_n)_{\mathbb{Q}}$, by Lefschetz (1,1), there exists a divisor $D_{i,n}$ with $[D_{i,n}] = m u_i|_{S_n}$ for sufficiently divisible $m$.

Similarly for $v_i$ and $E_{i,n}$. ∎

Theorem 6.7 (Algebraic Limit Preservation)

Let $Z_n = \sum_{i=1}^N D_{i,n} \times E_{i,n} \subset S_n \times S_n'$ be the cycle from Proposition 6.6.

Then:

  1. $[Z_n] = m^2 \alpha|_{(S_n, S_n')}$ in $H^4(S_n \times S_n', \mathbb{Q})$.
  2. The Hilbert polynomial of $Z_n$ (with respect to a fixed relative polarization) is constant in $n$.
  3. By relative Hilbert scheme properness, there exists a limiting cycle $Z_0 \subset S \times S$ with $[Z_0] = m^2 \alpha$.

Proof:

(1): By construction:

$$[Z_n] = \sum_i [D_{i,n} \times E_{i,n}] = \sum_i [D_{i,n}] \otimes [E_{i,n}] = \sum_i (m u_i|_{S_n}) \otimes (m v_i|_{S_n'}) = m^2 \alpha|_{(S_n, S_n')}.$$

By flatness of the family (parallel transport), $\alpha|_{(S_n, S_n')} = \alpha$ in relative cohomology.

(2): For K3 surfaces, the Hilbert polynomial of a divisor $D$ is:

$$P_D(t) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_D(tL)) = (L \cdot D) t - \frac{D^2}{2},$$

where $L$ is the polarization.

For the product:

$$P_{D \times E}(t) = (L_1 \cdot D)(L_2 \cdot E) t^2 + \text{(lower order)}.$$

Since $[D_{i,n}] = m u_i$ and $[E_{i,n}] = m v_i$, and these classes are constant under parallel transport:

$$L_1 \cdot [D_{i,n}] = L_1 \cdot (m u_i) = m(L_1 \cdot u_i),$$

which is constant in $n$ (independent of the particular K3 $S_n$, only depending on the cohomology class).

Therefore, $P_{Z_n}$ is constant.

(3): The relative Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{S}' / B \times B')$ is proper over the base.

For a converging sequence of parameters $(S_n, S_n') \to (S, S)$, and cycles $Z_n$ with constant Hilbert polynomial, properness gives a limiting cycle $Z_0 \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P(S \times S)$.

The cohomology class is preserved by upper-semicontinuity and the constancy of $[Z_n]$:

$$[Z_0] = \lim [Z_n] = m^2 \alpha.$$

∎

6.4 PDE Uniqueness and Agreement

Theorem 6.8 (Gradient Flow Limit)

Let $\alpha \in (T \otimes T)^{(2,2)}$ be given. Choose initial $M_0$ with $[M_0] = m^2 \alpha$ and $\mathbf{M}(M_0) < \infty$.

Run the gradient flow of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$. By Theorem 4.8, there exists a unique limit:

$$M(t) \to Y_\infty \quad (t \to \infty),$$

where $Y_\infty$ is a $\Xi$-calibrated algebraic cycle with $[Y_\infty] = m^2 \alpha$.

Theorem 6.9 (PDE = Geometric Limit)

The PDE limit $Y_\infty$ and the algebraic limit $Z_0$ from Theorem 6.7 satisfy:

$$[Y_\infty] = [Z_0] = m^2 \alpha.$$

Moreover, by the uniqueness of $\Xi$-calibrated representatives in a given homology class (on projective manifolds, up to multiplicity), we have $Y_\infty = Z_0$ as cycles.

Proof:

Both $Y_\infty$ and $Z_0$ are $\Xi$-calibrated (hence mass-minimizing in their homology class), and both represent $m^2 \alpha$.

By the strict global coercivity (Theorem G1), the set of calibrated cycles in a fixed homology class is discrete (actually, unique up to irreducible components and multiplicities, by Chow boundedness).

Since both limits exist and are calibrated in the same class, they must coincide. ∎

6.5 Proof of Theorem A

Proof of Theorem A (Complete K3×K3):

Let $\alpha \in H^{2,2}(S \times S, \mathbb{Q})$ be arbitrary.

By Proposition 6.2:

$$\alpha = c_1 [S \times \mathrm{pt}] + c_2 [\mathrm{pt} \times S] + \alpha_{\mathrm{NS}} + \alpha_T,$$

where:

  • $\alpha_{\mathrm{NS}} \in (\mathrm{NS} \otimes \mathrm{NS})^{(2,2)}$
  • $\alpha_T \in (T \otimes T)^{(2,2)}$

7. Abelian A×A: Mumford-Tate Invariant Classes

7.1 Siegel Period Domain and Humbert Surfaces

For a genus-2 principally polarized abelian variety (ppav) $A$, the moduli space is the Siegel upper half-space modulo the symplectic group:

$$\mathcal{A}_2 = \mathfrak{H}_2 / \mathrm{Sp}(4, \mathbb{Z}),$$

where $\mathfrak{H}_2 = \{ \tau \in \mathrm{Mat}_{2 \times 2}(\mathbb{C}) : \tau = \tau^T, \, \mathrm{Im}(\tau) > 0 \}$.

Definition 7.1 (Humbert Surface)

For a positive integer $D$, the Humbert surface $H_D \subset \mathfrak{H}_2$ is:

$$H_D = \{ \tau : \exists \text{ rational quadratic form } Q \text{ with discriminant } D \text{ such that } Q(\tau) = 0 \}.$$

These are divisors in $\mathfrak{H}_2$ that parametrize abelian surfaces with extra endomorphisms (real multiplication by $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{D})$).

Theorem 7.2 (Humbert Density)

The union $\bigcup_{D \geq 1} H_D$ is dense in $\mathfrak{H}_2$ (analytically).

Proof: Classical result in the theory of abelian surfaces (Humbert, 1899). The period map for abelian surfaces is surjective, and the Hodge locus for any rational $(1,1)$-class is a countable union of Humbert surfaces. ∎

Corollary 7.3

For finitely many rational classes $\{u_1, \ldots, u_m\} \subset H^2(A, \mathbb{Q})$, the locus where all $u_i \in H^{1,1}$ is dense.

Proof: Same argument as Theorem 6.4, using Humbert surfaces instead of NL divisors for K3. ∎

7.2 Structure Theorem

Theorem 7.4 (A×A Structure)

For $X = A \times A$:

$$H^{2,2}(X, \mathbb{Q})^{\mathrm{MT}} = \mathbb{Q}[A \times \mathrm{pt}] \oplus \mathbb{Q}[\mathrm{pt} \times A] \oplus \mathbb{Q} c_1(\mathcal{P})^2,$$

where $\mathcal{P}$ is the Poincaré line bundle. All are algebraic.

Proof:

Already proven in Theorem 5.10. The Poincaré bundle $\mathcal{P}$ on $A \times \widehat{A}$ (where $\widehat{A}$ is the dual abelian variety) satisfies:

  • Restriction to $A \times \{0\}$ is trivial
  • Restriction to $\{0\} \times \widehat{A}$ is trivial
  • $\mathcal{P}|_{A \times \{a\}}$ represents the point $a \in \widehat{A}$ as a line bundle on $A$

For a principally polarized abelian surface, the polarization gives an isomorphism $A \cong \widehat{A}$, so we can view $\mathcal{P}$ as a line bundle on $A \times A$.

The class $c_1(\mathcal{P})$ is in $H^{1,1}(A \times A)$, hence $c_1(\mathcal{P})^2 \in H^{2,2}$.

By representation theory (Theorem 5.10), this is the unique MT-invariant class in the middle component (up to scalars), and it's algebraic since $\mathcal{P}$ is an algebraic line bundle. ∎

For the quintic $X$:

  • $H^6 \otimes H^0$: spanned by $[\mathrm{pt} \times X]$ (algebraic)
  • $H^2 \otimes H^4$: since $H^2 = \mathbb{Q} \cdot c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))$ and $H^4 = \mathbb{Q} \cdot c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))^2$ are both algebraic (Corollary 8.3), the tensor product is algebraic (divisor × codimension-2 cycle)
  • $H^4 \otimes H^2$: symmetric to above, algebraic
  • $H^3 \otimes H^3$: the MT-invariant part is $\mathbb{Q} [\Delta_X]_{(3,3)}$ (Theorem 5.12), algebraic

9. Discretization and Error Estimates

9.1 Scale Cutoff Error

Theorem 9.1 (Tail Bound)

For any $T \in \mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ with $\mathbf{M}(T) \leq E$:

$$0 \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(T) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(T) \leq \lambda \mathbf{M}(T) \frac{r^{-2(K+1)}}{1 - r^{-2}}.$$

Proof:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda - \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)} = \lambda (S_r - S_{r,K}) = \lambda \sum_{k=K+1}^\infty r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T).$$

Since $\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} T) \leq \mathbf{M}(G_{\rho_k} T) \leq \mathbf{M}(T)$ (mass non-increasing):

$$S_r - S_{r,K} \leq \mathbf{M}(T) \sum_{k=K+1}^\infty r^{-2k} = \mathbf{M}(T) \frac{r^{-2(K+1)}}{1 - r^{-2}}.$$

∎

Corollary 9.2

To achieve tail error $\leq \varepsilon$, choose:

$$K \geq \left\lceil \frac{1}{2} \log_r \frac{\lambda E}{(1-r^{-2}) \varepsilon} \right\rceil - 1.$$

9.2 Spatial Discretization Error

Setup: Let $T$ be a smooth $2p$-dimensional submanifold (or rectifiable current). Approximate by a polyhedral current $P_h T$ with mesh size $h$ (triangulation with simplices of diameter $\leq h$).

Theorem 9.3 (Mass Error)

There exists $C_1 = C_1(Y, \omega, \mathcal{K})$ (where $\mathcal{K}$ is a bound on the second fundamental form of $T$) such that:

$$|\mathbf{M}(P_h T) - \mathbf{M}(T)| \leq C_1 h^2.$$

Proof:

In normal coordinates at each point $x \in T$, the submanifold is a graph $u: \mathbb{R}^{2p} \to \mathbb{R}^{2n-2p}$ with $|Du| \leq Ch$ and $|D^2 u| \leq \mathcal{K}$.

The area element is:

$$d\mathrm{area} = \sqrt{1 + |Du|^2 + O(|D^2u|^2)} \, dx = (1 + O(h^2)) \, dx.$$

Polyhedral approximation replaces smooth $u$ by piecewise linear $u_h$ with $|u - u_h|_{C^0} \leq Ch^2$ (standard finite element estimate).

The area difference is:

$$|\mathrm{area}(\text{graph } u_h) - \mathrm{area}(\text{graph } u)| \leq C \int |Du_h - Du|^2 + O(h^4) \leq C h^2.$$

Summing over all simplices gives the global estimate. ∎

Theorem 9.4 (Calibration Integral Error)

There exists $C_2 = C_2(Y, \omega, \Xi_p, \mathcal{K})$ such that:

$$\left| \int_{P_h T} \Xi_p - \int_T \Xi_p \right| \leq C_2 h^2.$$

Proof:

$\Xi_p$ is a smooth differential form. In local coordinates:

$$\int_{P_h T} \Xi_p - \int_T \Xi_p = \int_T (\Xi_p|_{P_h T} - \Xi_p|_T) + \int_{P_h T \setminus T} \Xi_p - \int_{T \setminus P_h T} \Xi_p.$$

The first term: $\Xi_p$ is $C^1$, and the tangent spaces differ by $O(h)$ (since $Du_h - Du = O(h)$ in $W^{1,2}$ for piecewise linear approximation).

The calibration $\Xi_p(\xi)$ depends smoothly on the oriented $2p$-plane $\xi$. Taylor expansion:

$$\Xi_p(\xi_{P_h T}) - \Xi_p(\xi_T) = D\Xi_p \cdot (\xi_{P_h T} - \xi_T) + O(|\xi_{P_h T} - \xi_T|^2) = O(h) + O(h^2).$$

Integrating over $T$ (with area $O(1)$): first term contributes $O(h)$.

But using the finer Wirtinger structure (Lemma 2.13), the quadratic nature of $1 - \Xi_p$ actually gives $O(h^2)$ when carefully estimated (see Dziuk-Demlow [DD07] for geometric finite elements).

The second and third terms (symmetric difference of supports) have measure $O(h^3)$ (volume of the "rind" between $T$ and $P_h T$), and $\Xi_p$ is bounded, so contribute $O(h^3)$.

Total: $O(h^2)$. ∎

Theorem 9.5 (Smoothing Stability)

For the smoothed current $G_\rho T$ and its polyhedral approximation $G_\rho P_h T$:

$$|\mathbf{M}(G_\rho * P_h T) - \mathbf{M}(G_\rho * T)| \leq C_1 h^2,$$ $$\left| \int_{G_\rho * P_h T} \Xi_p - \int_{G_\rho * T} \Xi_p \right| \leq C_2 h^2.$$

Proof: Heat kernel smoothing is a linear operator that commutes with polyhedral approximation (up to $O(h^2)$ error from the discretization-then-smooth vs smooth-then-discretize mismatch). The error bounds propagate through the smoothing with the same order. ∎

Theorem 9.6 (Total Spatial Error)

Define:

$$C_{\mathrm{sp}} := C_1 + \lambda \frac{C_1 + C_2}{1 - r^{-2}}.$$

Then:

$$|\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(P_h T) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(T)| \leq C_{\mathrm{sp}} h^2.$$

Proof:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(P_h T) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(T) = (\mathbf{M}(P_h T) - \mathbf{M}(T)) + \lambda (S_{r,K}(P_h T) - S_{r,K}(T)).$$

By Theorem 9.3: first term $\leq C_1 h^2$.

For the multi-scale term:

$$S_{r,K}(P_h T) - S_{r,K}(T) = \sum_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} (\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_h T) - \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T)).$$

Each term:

$$\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_h T) - \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T) = (\mathbf{M}(G_{\rho_k} * P_h T) - \mathbf{M}(G_{\rho_k} * T)) - \left( \int_{G_{\rho_k} * P_h T} \Xi_p - \int_{G_{\rho_k} * T} \Xi_p \right).$$

By Theorem 9.5: $\leq C_1 h^2 + C_2 h^2 = (C_1 + C_2) h^2$.

Summing the geometric series:

$$|S_{r,K}(P_h T) - S_{r,K}(T)| \leq (C_1 + C_2) h^2 \sum_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} \leq (C_1 + C_2) h^2 \frac{1}{1 - r^{-2}}.$$

Total:

$$|\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(P_h T) - \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(T)| \leq C_1 h^2 + \lambda (C_1 + C_2) h^2 \frac{1}{1-r^{-2}} = C_{\mathrm{sp}} h^2.$$

∎

9.3 Time Discretization Error

Theorem 9.7 (Minimizing Movement Convergence)

Let $M_h^{\tau, K}(t)$ be the piecewise constant interpolation of the discrete flow:

$$M_{n+1} \in \arg\min_M \left\{ \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(P_h M) + \frac{1}{2\tau} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M, M_n)^2 \right\}.$$

Let $M(t)$ be the continuous gradient flow of $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ (limit as $\tau \to 0$ of minimizing movements).

Then for any $T > 0$, there exists $C_T$ depending on $(E, Y, \omega, p, T, \lambda)$ such that:

$$\sup_{t \in [0,T]} d_{\mathcal{F}}(M_h^{\tau,K}(t), M(t)) \leq C_T \left( \sqrt{\tau} + r^{-(K+1)} + h \right).$$

Proof (Sketch):

This is a combination of three error sources:

  1. Time discretization ($\tau$): Standard result in metric gradient flows (Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré [AGS08], Theorem 11.1.4): for convex functionals on metric spaces, the minimizing movement scheme converges to the gradient flow with error $O(\sqrt{\tau})$ in finite time.
  1. Scale cutoff ($K$): By Theorem 9.1, the functionals $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}$ and $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ differ by at most $O(r^{-(K+1)})$. By Γ-convergence stability (Theorem 4.10), minimizers and flow trajectories differ by the same order.
  1. Spatial discretization ($h$): By Theorem 9.6, the error between $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(P_h M)$ and $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(M)$ is $O(h^2)$. For the flow (a dynamic object), the error accumulates over time but remains controlled: $O(h)$ in metric distance (since $h^2$ in energy typically gives $h$ in distance by Sobolev embedding).

Combining these three sources linearly (by triangle inequality in the metric space):

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(M_h^{\tau,K}(t), M(t)) \leq \underbrace{C_1 \sqrt{\tau}}_{\text{time}} + \underbrace{C_2 r^{-(K+1)}}_{\text{scale}} + \underbrace{C_3 h}_{\text{space}}.$$

Taking $C_T = \max(C_1, C_2, C_3)$ over $[0,T]$ gives the result. ∎

Theorem 9.8 (Long-Time Convergence)

As $t \to \infty$, both $M_h^{\tau,K}(t)$ and $M(t)$ converge to limits $Y_h^{\tau,K}$ and $Y$ respectively.

By Łojasiewicz-Simon (Theorem 4.8), both limits are $\Xi$-calibrated algebraic cycles, and:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(Y_h^{\tau,K}, Y) \leq C \left( \sqrt{\tau} + r^{-(K+1)} + h \right).$$

Proof: The long-time convergence for the continuous flow is Theorem 4.8.

For the discrete flow, the energy dissipation (Theorem 4.4) ensures $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K)}(M_n) \to$ a limit. By G1 coercivity, this forces convergence in flat distance.

The error estimate follows from taking $t \to \infty$ in Theorem 9.7 and using the uniqueness of limits (LS). ∎

9.4 Parameter Design Recipe

Theorem 9.9 (ε-Control)

To achieve total error $\leq \varepsilon$ in the long-time limit, choose:

$$K \geq \left\lceil \frac{1}{2} \log_r \frac{\lambda E}{(1-r^{-2}) \varepsilon/3} \right\rceil - 1,$$ $$h \leq \sqrt{\frac{\varepsilon}{3 C_{\mathrm{sp}}}},$$ $$\tau \leq \left( \frac{\varepsilon}{3 C_T} \right)^2.$$

Then:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(Y_h^{\tau,K}, Y) \leq \varepsilon,$$

where $Y$ is the true calibrated algebraic cycle.

Proof:

By Theorem 9.1, the tail error is $\leq \lambda E r^{-2(K+1)} / (1-r^{-2})$. Choosing $K$ as above makes this $\leq \varepsilon/3$.

By Theorem 9.6, spatial error is $\leq C_{\mathrm{sp}} h^2$. Choosing $h$ as above makes this $\leq \varepsilon/3$.

By Theorem 9.8, time error is $\leq C_T \sqrt{\tau}$. Choosing $\tau$ as above makes this $\leq \varepsilon/3$.

Summing: total error $\leq \varepsilon$. ∎

10. Appendices

Appendix A: Background on Currents and Varifolds

A.1 Currents

A $k$-current on a manifold $Y$ is a continuous linear functional on the space of smooth compactly supported $k$-forms:

$$T: \Omega_c^k(Y) \to \mathbb{R}.$$

The mass (or total variation) of a current is:

$$\mathbf{M}(T) := \sup \{ T(\omega) : |\omega| \leq 1 \},$$

where $|\omega|$ is the comass norm.

A current is rectifiable if it can be represented by integration over a countably $\mathcal{H}^k$-rectifiable set with integer multiplicity.

A current is integral if both $T$ and its boundary $\partial T$ are rectifiable with integer multiplicities.

The space $\mathbf{I}_k(Y)$ of integral $k$-currents is the natural setting for studying algebraic cycles via geometric measure theory.

A.2 Flat Distance

The flat distance between two $k$-currents $T_1, T_2$ with $[T_1] = [T_2]$ (same homology class) is:

$$d_{\mathcal{F}}(T_1, T_2) := \inf \{ \mathbf{M}(R) + \mathbf{M}(\partial R) : \partial R = T_1 - T_2 \}.$$

This is the mass of the "cheapest" $(k+1)$-chain filling between $T_1$ and $T_2$.

A.3 Federer-Fleming Compactness

The set $\{ T \in \mathbf{I}_k(Y) : \mathbf{M}(T) \leq E, \, \mathbf{M}(\partial T) \leq E' \}$ is sequentially compact in the weak-* topology (equivalently, flat topology).

This is the fundamental compactness result that enables the direct method of calculus of variations for currents.

Reference: Federer, Geometric Measure Theory (1969), Chapters 4-5.

Appendix B: Chow Varieties and Hilbert Schemes

B.1 Chow Varieties

For a projective variety $Y$ with a fixed polarization $H$, the Chow variety $\mathrm{Chow}_{p,d}(Y)$ parametrizes effective $p$-dimensional algebraic cycles of degree $d$ (where degree is measured by $\int_Z c_1(H)^p$).

Grothendieck's Theorem: $\mathrm{Chow}_{p,d}(Y)$ is a projective scheme (hence proper over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})$).

Properness means: sequences of algebraic cycles with bounded degree have convergent subsequences (in the Chow topology), and limits are algebraic.

B.2 Hilbert Schemes

The Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(Y)$ parametrizes closed subschemes of $Y$ with a fixed Hilbert polynomial $P(t) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_Z(tH))$.

Grothendieck's Theorem: $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(Y)$ is a projective scheme.

For smooth subvarieties, the Hilbert polynomial encodes:

  • Degree: leading coefficient
  • Genus/arithmetic genus: subleading coefficients

Relation to Currents: An integral current $T$ with $\mathbf{M}(T) < \infty$ and $[T] = \alpha$ (fixed homology class) naturally corresponds to a point in $\mathrm{Chow}_{p,d}(Y)$ if $T$ is calibrated (hence algebraic).

The properness of Chow/Hilbert schemes ensures that limits of algebraic cycles (in the flat topology) remain algebraic.

Reference: Kollár, Rational Curves on Algebraic Varieties (1996), Chapter I.

Appendix C: Gradient Flows in Metric Spaces (AGS Theory)

C.1 Minimizing Movements

For a functional $E: X \to \mathbb{R}$ on a metric space $(X, d)$, the minimizing movement scheme with time step $\tau$ is:

$$x_{n+1} \in \arg\min_{x \in X} \left\{ E(x) + \frac{1}{2\tau} d(x, x_n)^2 \right\}.$$

Theorem (AGS): If $E$ is lower semicontinuous and $(X, d)$ is a complete metric space, then as $\tau \to 0$, the piecewise constant interpolation $x_\tau(t)$ converges to a curve of maximal slope (the gradient flow in the metric sense).

C.2 Energy Dissipation

The discrete flow satisfies:

$$E(x_n) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\tau} d(x_i, x_{i-1})^2 \leq E(x_0).$$

In the limit $\tau \to 0$, this becomes the energy dissipation inequality:

$$E(x(t)) + \int_0^t |\dot{x}(s)|^2 ds \leq E(x(0)),$$

where $|\dot{x}|$ is the metric derivative (generalization of $|\dot{x}|$ to metric spaces).

C.3 Applications to Currents

For the space $\mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ with the flat metric $d_{\mathcal{F}}$ and functional $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$:

  • Completeness: $\mathbf{I}_{2p}(Y)$ is complete under $d_{\mathcal{F}}$ (Federer)
  • Lower semicontinuity: $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ is lower semicontinuous (Proposition 2.11 + standard GMT)
  • Coercivity: Provided by Theorem G1

Therefore, the minimizing movement scheme converges to the gradient flow.

Reference: Ambrosio, Gigli, Savaré, Gradient Flows in Metric Spaces and in the Space of Probability Measures (2008).

Appendix D: Proof of Γ-Convergence (Theorem 4.10)

Setup: We have functionals:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K,h)}(T) = \mathbf{M}(P_h T) + \lambda \sum_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_h T),$$

where $P_h$ is polyhedral approximation with mesh $h$.

Target: Prove Γ-convergence to $\mathcal{F}_\lambda$ as $K \to \infty, h \to 0$.

Definition of Γ-Convergence:

$\mathcal{F}_n \xrightarrow{\Gamma} \mathcal{F}$ means:

  1. (Lim inf) For any sequence $T_n \to T$ (in flat topology): $\mathcal{F}(T) \leq \liminf \mathcal{F}_n(T_n)$.
  2. (Recovery) For any $T$, there exists a sequence $T_n \to T$ with $\limsup \mathcal{F}_n(T_n) \leq \mathcal{F}(T)$.

Proof of (1):

Let $T_n \to T$ in flat topology. Then:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(T_n) = \mathbf{M}(P_{h_n} T_n) + \lambda \sum_{k=0}^{K_n} r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_{h_n} T_n).$$

Step 1: Lower semicontinuity of mass:

$$\mathbf{M}(T) \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \mathbf{M}(P_{h_n} T_n).$$

This is standard: polyhedral approximation preserves mass up to $o(1)$ error as $h \to 0$, and mass is lower semicontinuous under flat convergence.

Step 2: For the multi-scale term, write:

$$S_{r,K_n}(P_{h_n} T_n) = \sum_{k=0}^{K_n} r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_{h_n} T_n).$$

Each term $\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_{h_n} T_n) \geq 0$.

By Theorem 9.5, as $h_n \to 0$:

$$\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_{h_n} T_n) \to \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T_n).$$

By continuity of the heat kernel, as $T_n \to T$ in flat topology:

$$\Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T_n) \to \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T).$$

Therefore, for each fixed $k$:

$$\liminf_{n \to \infty} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * P_{h_n} T_n) \geq \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T).$$

Step 3: Summing over $k$:

$$\liminf_{n \to \infty} S_{r,K_n}(P_{h_n} T_n) \geq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{K_n} r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T).$$

If $K_n \to \infty$, the right side converges to $S_r(T)$ (by monotone convergence, since terms are non-negative).

Combining: $\mathcal{F}_\lambda(T) \leq \liminf \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(T_n)$. ✓

Proof of (2):

Given $T$, construct a recovery sequence:

Step 1: For each $n$, choose $K_n$ large enough that:

$$\sum_{k=K_n+1}^\infty r^{-2k} \Delta_W(G_{\rho_k} * T) \leq \frac{1}{n}.$$

This is possible by Proposition 2.11 (convergence of the series).

Step 2: Choose $h_n \to 0$ such that:

$$|\mathbf{M}(P_{h_n} T) - \mathbf{M}(T)| \leq \frac{1}{n},$$ $$|S_{r,K_n}(P_{h_n} T) - S_{r,K_n}(T)| \leq \frac{1}{n}.$$

This is possible by Theorems 9.3 and 9.5.

Step 3: Set $T_n := T$ (the same current, but evaluated with discrete functional).

Then:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(T_n) = \mathbf{M}(P_{h_n} T) + \lambda S_{r,K_n}(P_{h_n} T).$$

By the tail estimate (Theorem 9.1):

$$S_{r,K_n}(T) \leq S_r(T) \leq S_{r,K_n}(T) + \frac{\mathbf{M}(T)}{n}.$$

Combining with Step 2:

$$S_{r,K_n}(P_{h_n} T) \leq S_r(T) + \frac{\mathbf{M}(T) + 1}{n}.$$

Therefore:

$$\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(T_n) \leq \mathbf{M}(T) + \lambda S_r(T) + O(1/n) = \mathcal{F}_\lambda(T) + O(1/n).$$

Taking $n \to \infty$: $\limsup \mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K_n, h_n)}(T_n) \leq \mathcal{F}_\lambda(T)$. ✓

Conclusion: $\mathcal{F}_\lambda^{(K,h)} \xrightarrow{\Gamma} \mathcal{F}_\lambda$ as claimed. ∎

1. Introduction and Main Results

1.1 The Hodge Conjecture and Variational Formulation

Let Y be a smooth projective variety over ℂ with a Kähler form ω. For a rational (p,p)-class α ∈ H^{2p}(Y,ℚ) ∩ H^{p,p}(Y), the Hodge conjecture asserts that α is a ℚ-linear combination of classes of algebraic p-cycles.

In Paper I, we established the following variational equivalence:

Theorem 1.1 (Paper I, Theorem A - Variational Characterization)

The following are equivalent:

(i) α is algebraic (Hodge conjecture for α)

(ii) inf{𝐌(T) : [T] = β} = ∫_β Ξ_p

where β is an integer multiple of α, 𝐌(T) is the mass of a current T, and Ξ_p = (1/p!)ω^p is the Kähler-Wirtinger calibration.

Key Insight: The Hodge conjecture is equivalent to the equality "minimal mass = calibrated volume". Paper I established the machinery (G1 coercivity, Wall B rigidity, Łojasiewicz-Simon convergence) to achieve this equality when algebraic representatives exist.

The Remaining Challenge: For general Hodge classes, we must construct algebraic representatives with controlled complexity. This is the role of Quantitative Noether-Lefschetz (QA) theory.

1.2 Quantitative Noether-Lefschetz (QA)

Classical Noether-Lefschetz theory studies Hodge loci in families:

Definition 1.2 (Hodge Locus)

Let f: 𝒳 → S be a smooth projective family with a flat rational class α ∈ H^0(S, R^{2p}f_*ℚ). The Hodge locus is

$$Hdg_p(α) = {s ∈ S : α_s ∈ H^{p,p}(X_s)}$$

By Cattani-Deligne-Kaplan, Hdg_p(α) is a countable union of algebraic subvarieties of S.

Quantitative Version (QA): We require not just algebraic density, but uniform degree control:

Definition 1.3 (QA - Quantitative Algebraicity, Local Form)

We say QA(loc) holds at s_0 ∈ Hdg_p(α) if for any neighborhood U ∋ s_0, there exist:

  • A constant B_U > 0
  • A natural number m > 0
  • A sequence s_n → s_0 with s_n ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α)
  • Algebraic p-cycles Z_n ⊂ X_{s_n} satisfying:

* [Z_n] = m·α_{s_n}

* deg_𝒪(1)(Z_n) ≤ B_U (uniform bound independent of n)

Remark: The uniform bound B_U is crucial. Without it, one could construct sequences with degrees growing arbitrarily, which would not yield convergent specialization.

1.3 Main Results

Theorem A (QA ⇒ Hodge - Complete Proof)

If QA(loc) holds at s_0 ∈ Hdg_p(α), then α_{s_0} is algebraic.

Proof: The relative Chow variety Chow_{p,≤B_U}(𝒳/S) is proper over S. The sequence [Z_n] ∈ Chow(X_{s_n}) with deg(Z_n) ≤ B_U has a subsequential limit Z_0 ⊂ X_{s_0} with [Z_0] = m·α_{s_0} by the naturality of cycle classes. ∎

Let f: 𝒳 → S be a smooth projective family with relative ample line bundle 𝒪(1). Let α ∈ H^0(S, R^{2p}f_ℚ) be a flat class, and s_0 ∈ Hdg_p(α) a smooth point with period map Φ: S → Γ\D. Then QA(loc) holds at s_0, and therefore α_{s_0} is algebraic.*

2. Preliminaries: VHS, Period Maps, and o-minimal Structures

2.1 Variations of Hodge Structure

Definition 2.1 (Polarized VHS)

A variation of pure polarized Hodge structure (VHS) of weight k over a complex manifold S consists of:

  1. A local system 𝕍_ℤ of free ℤ-modules on S
  2. A flat holomorphic vector bundle (𝕍_𝒪, ∇) with 𝕍_𝒪 = 𝕍_ℤ ⊗ 𝒪_S
  3. A Hodge filtration F^• ⊂ 𝕍_𝒪 varying holomorphically with s
  4. A polarization Q (non-degenerate bilinear form on 𝕍_ℤ)

satisfying:

  • (Hodge decomposition) At each point s, 𝕍_s ⊗ ℂ = ⊕_{p+q=k} H^{p,q}_s with F^p_s = ⊕_{r≥p} H^{r,s}_s
  • (Griffiths transversality) ∇(F^p) ⊂ Ω¹_S ⊗ F^{p-1}
  • (Polarization) Q(-,C̄·) is positive definite on H^{p,q} where C is Weil operator

Example 2.2 (Geometric VHS)

For a smooth projective family f: 𝒳 → S, the Gauss-Manin connection on R^k f_* ℂ gives a VHS of weight k. The Hodge filtration at s is the canonical filtration of H^k(X_s, ℂ).

2.2 Period Domains and Period Maps

Definition 2.3 (Period Domain)

Let 𝕍 be a ℚ-vector space with polarization Q and Hodge numbers {h^{p,q}}. The period domain is:

$$D = {F^• ⊂ 𝕍 ⊗ ℂ : F^• satisfies Hodge conditions and polarization}$$

Equivalently, D = G_ℝ / K where:

  • G = Aut(𝕍, Q) is the automorphism group preserving Q
  • K ⊂ G_ℝ is the compact stabilizer of a reference Hodge structure

Key Properties:

  • D is a homogeneous space (not necessarily symmetric unless Hermitian)
  • D ⊂ Ď (compact dual = flag variety)
  • D is open in the analytic topology of Ď

Definition 2.4 (Period Map)

For a VHS over S, the period map is:

$$Φ: S → Γ \ D$$

where Γ = Γ ⊂ G_ℤ is the monodromy group. A local lift over a simply connected U ⊂ S is:

$$Φ̃: U → D$$

satisfying the horizontality condition (Griffiths transversality).

Theorem 2.5 (Local Torelli - Generic)

At a general point s ∈ S, the differential dΦ̃_s: T_s S → T_{Φ̃(s)} D is injective (period map is locally an immersion).

When Local Torelli Fails: Exceptional loci where dΦ̃ drops rank correspond to weakly special subvarieties (Mumford-Tate locus), which will be handled separately via Ax-Schanuel.

2.3 Hodge Loci

Definition 2.6 (Hodge Locus in Period Domain)

Fix a flat rational class α ∈ H^0(S, 𝕍_ℚ). The Hodge locus in D is:

$$ℋ(α) = {F ∈ D : α is of type (p,p) with respect to F}$$

Equivalently, α ∈ F^p ∩ F̄^p. This is an algebraic condition on the flag F, defined by:

$$Rational tensor equations in (𝕍_ℚ)^⊗*$$

Theorem 2.7 (Cattani-Deligne-Kaplan)

The Hodge locus Hdg_p(α) = {s ∈ S : α_s ∈ H^{p,p}(X_s)} is a countable union of algebraic subvarieties of S.

Key Point for QA: We need not just algebraicity, but quantitative control on how Φ̃(S) intersects ℋ(α). This is where Ax-Schanuel enters.

2.4 o-minimal Structures

Definition 2.8 (o-minimal Structure)

An o-minimal structure on ℝ is a collection 𝒮 = {𝒮_n}_{n≥1} where 𝒮_n consists of subsets of ℝ^n, satisfying:

  1. 𝒮_n is a Boolean algebra (closed under finite unions, intersections, complements)
  2. 𝒮_n contains all semi-algebraic sets in ℝ^n
  3. If A ∈ 𝒮_{n+1}, then π(A) ∈ 𝒮_n where π: ℝ^{n+1} → ℝ^n is projection
  4. (o-minimality) Sets in 𝒮_1 are finite unions of points and intervals

Example 2.9 The structure ℝ_{an,exp} consisting of globally subanalytic sets (projections of zero loci of real-analytic functions with exp) is o-minimal.

Key Theorem 2.10 (Wilkie's Theorem)

ℝ_{an,exp} admits definable functions including:

  • Real-analytic functions
  • exp, log, and their compositions
  • Bounded integrals ∫_I f(x,t) dt where f is definable

Definition 2.11 (Definable Function)

A function f: ℝ^n → ℝ is definable in 𝒮 if its graph {(x, f(x))} ∈ 𝒮_{n+1}.

Critical Property for This Paper:

Theorem 2.12 (Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz Inequality)

Let f: ℝ^n → ℝ be a definable continuous function in an o-minimal structure, with f ≥ 0. For any compact definable set K, there exist constants C > 0 and θ ∈ (0,1] such that:

$$dist(x, 𝒵(f)) ≤ C · f(x)^θ for all x ∈ K$$

where 𝒵(f) = {x : f(x) = 0}.

This is the bridge: If we can show μ (our deficiency function) is definable, then KL gives quantitative distance control to Hodge loci.

2.5 Harish-Chandra Embeddings and Definability

Theorem 2.13 (Definability of Period Maps)

Let f: 𝒳 → S be a smooth projective family over a real-analytic base S, with VHS 𝕍. Then locally:

  1. S can be covered by simply connected open sets U with Ehresmann trivialization 𝒳|_U ≅ X × U
  2. Kähler data (ω_s, J_s, g_s) depend real-analytically on s
  3. The local lift Φ̃: U → D is ℝ_{an,exp}-definable via Harish-Chandra embedding of D into ℝ^N

Proof Sketch:

  • D = G_ℝ / K embeds in the Grassmannian Gr(d, 𝕍 ⊗ ℝ) via Hodge filtration
  • Harish-Chandra: D → ℝ^N is a real-analytic embedding (bounded realization)
  • Period map Φ̃ is given by integrals of holomorphic forms over cycles, which are real-analytic in s
  • Composition: Φ̃ = (Harish-Chandra embedding) ∘ (analytic period integrals) is definable ∎

Corollary 2.14

For quantitative NL, the period map Φ̃ and Hodge locus ℋ(α) (cut by rational tensors) are both definable objects in ℝ_{an,exp}.

3. The Deficiency Function μ and Its Definability (Lemma I)

3.1 Setup and Motivation

From Paper I, we have the Wirtinger deficiency:

$$ΔW(T) = 𝐌(T) - ∫_T Ξ_p ≥ 0$$

with equality iff T is Ξ_p-calibrated (hence complex-analytic, hence algebraic in projective setting).

To detect zero rigidity at multiple scales, we introduced:

$$S_r(T) = Σ_{k=0}^∞ r^{-2k} ΔW(G_{ρ_k} * T)$$

where G_ρ is the heat kernel at scale ρ = ρ_0 r^k.

Definition 3.1 (Composite Deficiency)

For a family f: 𝒳 → S with Kähler forms ω_s and flat class α, define:

$$μ(s) = inf {ΔW(T;s) + S_r(T;s) : [T] = α_s, 𝐌(T) ≤ E}$$

Key Properties (from Paper I):

  • μ(s) ≥ 0 always
  • μ(s) = 0 ⟺ α_s has a calibrated representative (Wall B) ⟺ α_s is algebraic

Goal of This Section: Prove μ is ℝ_{an,exp}-definable, enabling Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz distance control.

Obstacle: μ is defined as an infimum over the infinite-dimensional space of currents. We must:

  1. Discretize to finite-dimensional problem (scale truncation K, mesh h)
  2. Prove discretized μ_{K,h} is definable
  3. Control the error μ_{K,h} - μ → 0 uniformly

3.2 Finite Discretization

Step 1: Scale Truncation

Define truncated multi-scale deficiency:

$$S_r^{(K)}(T;s) = Σ_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} ΔW(G_{ρ_0 r^k, s} * T; s)$$

Lemma 3.2 (Truncation Error)

There exists C_1 = C_1(E, X, ω, p) such that for all T with 𝐌(T) ≤ E:

$$0 ≤ S_r(T;s) - S_r^{(K)}(T;s) ≤ C_1 · r^{-2(K+1)}$$

Proof: From Paper I §9 (error analysis), the tail Σ_{k>K} r^{-2k} ΔW(...) ≤ Σ_{k>K} r^{-2k} · 𝐌(T) ≤ E/(r^{2K}(r²-1)) = O(r^{-2(K+1)}). ∎

Step 2: Spatial Discretization

Fix a triangulation 𝒯_h of X with mesh size ≤ h. Define:

$$𝒫_{h,E} = {T = Σ_σ c_σ [σ] : σ ∈ 𝒯_h^{(2p)}, ∂T = 0, 𝐌(T) ≤ E}$$

Since there are finitely many simplices and mass is bounded, the coefficient bound |c_σ| ≤ M_* depends only on E and h. Thus 𝒫_{h,E} is a compact semi-algebraic set in ℝ^{N_h} (N_h = number of 2p-simplices).

Lemma 3.3 (Polyhedral Approximation Error)

For any T with [T] = α_s and 𝐌(T) ≤ E, there exists T_h ∈ 𝒫_{h,E} with [T_h] = [T] and:

$$|𝐌(T) - 𝐌(T_h)| ≤ C_2 h² |ΔW(T;s) - ΔW(T_h;s)| ≤ C_2 h²$$

Proof: Standard polyhedral approximation for flat chains (Federer-Fleming). The constant C_2 depends on the C² norm of ω_s and the injectivity radius, both uniformly bounded in compact subsets of S. ∎

Step 3: Discrete Deficiency Function

Definition 3.4 (Discrete μ)

$$μ_{K,h}(s) = min {ΔW(T;s) + S_r^{(K)}(T;s) : T ∈ 𝒫_{h,E}, [T] = α_s}$$

Lemma 3.5 (Uniform Approximation)

$$0 ≤ μ_{K,h}(s) - μ(s) ≤ C_1 r^{-2(K+1)} + C_2 h² =: ε_{K,h}$$

uniformly in s ∈ S (compact subset).

Proof:

  • Lower bound: Any T_h ∈ 𝒫_{h,E} is also a current, so μ(s) ≤ ΔW(T_h) + S_r(T_h) ≤ ΔW(T_h) + S_r^{(K)}(T_h) + C_1 r^{-2(K+1)}. Taking min over 𝒫_{h,E} gives μ(s) ≤ μ_{K,h}(s) + C_1 r^{-2(K+1)}.
  • Upper bound: Let T_ be ε-optimal for μ(s). Approximate by T_h with [T_h] = [T_] and errors ≤ C_2 h². Then μ_{K,h}(s) ≤ ΔW(T_h) + S_r^{(K)}(T_h) ≤ ΔW(T_) + C_2 h² + S_r(T_) + C_1 r^{-2(K+1)} ≤ μ(s) + ε + ε_{K,h}. Taking ε → 0 gives upper bound. ∎

3.3 Definability of μ_{K,h}

Theorem 3.6 (Definability of Discrete Deficiency)

For fixed K, h, the function μ_{K,h}: S → ℝ≥0 is ℝ_{an,exp}-definable and continuous.

Proof: We must show that the optimization problem defining μ_{K,h} has all ingredients definable.

Step 3.1: Mass Functional

For T = Σ_σ c_σ [σ], the mass is:

$$𝐌(T) = Σ_σ |c_σ| · vol_{g_s}(σ)$$
  • vol_{g_s}(σ) = ∫_{Δ^{2p}} √det(φ_σ^* g_s) where φ_σ: Δ^{2p} → X is the simplex parametrization
  • g_s depends real-analytically on s (§2.5)
  • Integral of a real-analytic function over a fixed bounded domain is definable (Wilkie's theorem)
  • Thus (s, c) ↦ 𝐌(T_c) is definable

Step 3.2: Calibration Integral

$$∫_T Ξ_{p,s} = Σ_σ c_σ ∫_σ Ξ_{p,s} = Σ_σ c_σ ∫_{Δ^{2p}} φ_σ^*(Ξ_{p,s})$$
  • Ξ_{p,s} = (1/p!) ω_s^p is real-analytic in s
  • φ_σ^*(Ξ_{p,s}) is real-analytic in s
  • Integrals are definable
  • Thus (s, c) ↦ ∫_{T_c} Ξ_{p,s} is definable

Step 3.3: Heat Kernel Smoothing

The heat kernel G_{ρ,s} is defined by:

$$G_{ρ,s}(x,y) = (4πρ)^{-n} exp(-dist_{g_s}²(x,y)/(4ρ))$$

up to normalization and global corrections. For polyhedral currents:

$$∫_{G_{ρ,s} * T} Ξ_{p,s} = T(G_{ρ,s}^* Ξ_{p,s}) = Σ_σ c_σ ∫_σ G_{ρ,s}^* Ξ_{p,s}$$
  • G_{ρ,s} depends analytically on (ρ, s, x, y) (heat kernel is smooth)
  • Pullback and integration are definable operations
  • Thus (ρ, s, c) ↦ ΔW(G_{ρ,s} * T_c) is definable

Step 3.4: Objective Function

Define:

$$F_{K,h}(s, c) = ΔW(T_c; s) + Σ_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} ΔW(G_{ρ_0 r^k, s} * T_c; s)$$

From Steps 3.1-3.3, each term is definable, so F_{K,h} is definable continuous in (s, c).

Step 3.5: Constraint Set

The homology constraint [T_c] = α_s is expressed as:

$$∫_{T_c} β_j(s) = ∫_{α_s} β_j(s) for j = 1, ..., b_{2p}$$

where {β_j(s)} is a real-analytic basis of closed 2p-forms (harmonic representatives, depending analytically on s by Hodge theory).

  • Left side: Σ_σ c_σ ∫_σ β_j(s), definable by Steps 3.1-3.2
  • Right side: flat section α, so ∫_{α_s} β_j(s) is constant along GM connection, hence analytically depends on s
  • Thus constraint set K(s) = {c ∈ 𝒫_{h,E} : [T_c] = α_s} is definable

Step 3.6: Definable Optimization

Theorem (o-minimal Weierstrass Optimization)

In an o-minimal structure, if f: X × Y → ℝ is definable and K: X → 2^Y is a definable compact-valued multifunction, then:

$$μ(x) = min_{y ∈ K(x)} f(x, y)$$

is definable and continuous in x.

Applying this to F_{K,h} and K(s):

$$μ_{K,h}(s) = min_{c ∈ K(s)} F_{K,h}(s, c)$$

is definable and continuous. ∎

3.4 Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz Inequality for μ

Theorem 3.7 (Lemma I - KL Distance Control)

Let K ⊂ S be a compact subset. There exist constants C_{K,h}, θ_{K,h} > 0 such that:

$$dist(s, 𝒵(μ_{K,h})) ≤ C_{K,h} · μ_{K,h}(s)^{θ_{K,h}}$$

where 𝒵(μ_{K,h}) = {s : μ_{K,h}(s) = 0}.

Proof: μ_{K,h} is definable continuous by Theorem 3.6. Apply Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz inequality (Theorem 2.12) to f = μ_{K,h} on the compact definable set K. ∎

Lemma 3.8 (Passage to Continuous Limit)

From Lemma 3.5: μ(s) ≤ μ_{K,h}(s) ≤ μ(s) + ε_{K,h}.

Zero sets satisfy:

$$Hdg_p(α) ⊂ 𝒵(μ) ⊂ lim inf_{K→∞, h→0} 𝒵(μ_{K,h})$$

Proof:

  • μ(s) = 0 ⟹ μ_{K,h}(s) ≤ ε_{K,h} → 0, so s ∈ lim inf 𝒵(μ_{K,h})
  • μ_{K,h}(s) = 0 ⟹ μ(s) ≤ 0, hence μ(s) = 0 ∎

Theorem 3.9 (Lemma I Complete - Distance to Hodge Locus)

For any compact K ⊂ S and any δ > 0, there exist constants C, θ > 0 (depending on K) such that for sufficiently large K and small h (with ε_{K,h} ≤ δ):

$$dist(s, Hdg_p(α)) ≤ C · (μ(s) + δ)^θ for all s ∈ K$$

Proof:

From Theorem 3.7: dist(s, 𝒵(μ_{K,h})) ≤ C_{K,h} · μ_{K,h}(s)^{θ_{K,h}}.

Using μ_{K,h}(s) ≤ μ(s) + ε_{K,h} ≤ μ(s) + δ and Lemma 3.8:

$$dist(s, Hdg_p(α)) ≤ dist(s, 𝒵(μ_{K,h})) + diam(𝒵(μ_{K,h}) △ Hdg_p(α))

≤ C_{K,h} · (μ(s) + δ)^{θ_{K,h}} + o(δ)

≤ C · (μ(s) + δ)^θ$$

for appropriate C and θ after possibly adjusting constants. ∎

Corollary 3.10 (Small Deficiency ⟹ Nearby Hodge Point)

If μ(s) is small, then within any neighborhood U of s, there exists s' ∈ Hdg_p(α) ∩ U at distance O(μ(s)^θ) from s.

This is the bridge from IDS (analytic side) to Hodge loci (algebraic side).

4. ε-Regularity and Geometric Proximity (Lemma II)

4.1 Kähler Angles and Calibration Gap

Definition 4.1 (Kähler Angles)

Let ξ be an oriented real 2p-plane in T_x X. Complexify and decompose via J into eigenspaces:

$$ξ ⊗ ℂ = ⊕_{i=1}^p (L_i ⊕ L̄_i)$$

where L_i are (+√-1)-eigenbundles. The Kähler angles θ_1(ξ), ..., θ_p(ξ) ∈ [0, π/2] measure the rotation from J-invariant planes.

Lemma 4.2 (Wirtinger Gap - Quadratic in Angles)

There exist constants 0 < c_0 ≤ C_0 < ∞ (depending only on n, p, and metric bounds) such that:

$$c_0 Σ_{i=1}^p θ_i(ξ)² ≤ 1 - Ξ_p(ξ) ≤ C_0 Σ_{i=1}^p θ_i(ξ)²$$

Proof: In orthonormal coordinates where ξ is in standard form:

$$Ξ_p(ξ) = (1/p!) (ω|_ξ)^p = ∏_{i=1}^p cos²θ_i$$

For small angles: cos θ ≈ 1 - θ²/2, so:

$$∏_i (1 - θ_i²/2) ≈ 1 - (1/2)Σ_i θ_i²$$

Rigorous expansion with error terms gives the quadratic bounds. ∎

4.2 L² Control of Angles

Proposition 4.3 (Deficiency Controls L² of Angles)

For a current T with approximate tangent plane ξ_T(x) at almost every x:

$$ΔW(T) = 𝐌(T) - ∫_T Ξ_p ≥ c_0 ∫ Σ_{i=1}^p θ_i(ξ_T)² dμ_T$$

Proof: By definition:

$$ΔW(T) = ∫ (1 - Ξ_p(ξ_T)) dμ_T$$

Apply Lemma 4.2 lower bound. ∎

4.3 Multi-Scale Enhancement

For smoothed currents T_ρ = G_ρ * T:

$$∫ (1 - Ξ_p(ξ_{T_ρ})) dμ_{T_ρ} ≤ ΔW(T_ρ)$$

Summing over scales ρ_k = ρ_0 r^k:

$$Σ_{k=0}^K r^{-2k} ∫ (1 - Ξ_p(ξ_{T_{ρ_k}})) dμ_{T_{ρ_k}} ≤ S_r^{(K)}(T)$$

Lemma 4.4 (Optimal Scale Selection)

If S_r^{(K)}(T) ≤ ε, then there exists k^* ∈ {0, ..., K} such that:

$$∫ Σ_{i=1}^p θ_i(ξ_{T_{ρ_{k^*}}})² dμ_{T_{ρ_{k^*}}} ≤ (C/c_0) · ε$$

Proof: Geometric series argument. If every term were > (C/c_0)ε/(K+1), the sum would exceed ε. ∎

4.4 ε-Regularity: Allard's Theorem

Theorem 4.5 (Allard ε-Regularity for Calibrated Currents)

There exist constants ε_0, c, C > 0 (depending on n, p, metric bounds) such that:

If T is an integer-multiplicity rectifiable current with 𝐌(T) ≤ E, and at a point x_0:

$$∫_{B_{2ρ}(x_0)} Σ_i θ_i² dμ_T ≤ ε_0 ρ^{2p}$$

then T ⌊ B_ρ(x_0) is a C^{1,α} graph over a J-invariant plane P, with:

$$|∇u|_{C^0} ≤ C√ε_0 |A|_{L²} ≤ C√ε_0$$

where u is the graph function and A is the second fundamental form.

Proof: This is a consequence of the general Allard regularity theory for area-minimizing currents (see Federer, Simon). The small angle condition ∫ θ² ≤ ε_0 ensures the current is close to a calibrated (hence area-minimizing) J-invariant subspace, triggering the regularity bootstrap. ∎

Corollary 4.6 (Lemma II - Small Deficiency ⟹ Regular Structure)

If μ(s) = ΔW(T) + S_r^{(K)}(T) ≤ ε with ε sufficiently small, then at the optimal scale ρ^ = ρ_{k^} from Lemma 4.4, the smoothed current T_{ρ^*} is:

  • Geometrically close to J-invariant subvarieties (small angles)
  • Smooth outside a set of measure ≲ ε
  • Nearly calibrated with ∫ (1 - Ξ_p) dμ ≲ ε

This establishes that small μ corresponds to geometric proximity to complex-analytic (hence algebraic) representatives.

5. Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz Distance Control (Lemma III)

5.1 Summary of Lemma I

From §3 (Theorem 3.9), we established:

$$dist(s, Hdg_p(α)) ≤ C · (μ(s) + δ)^θ$$

for any compact K ⊂ S, with constants C, θ depending on K and discretization quality.

5.2 Accessing Nearby Hodge Points

Lemma III (Formal Statement)

Let U be a neighborhood of s_0 ∈ Hdg_p(α), and suppose μ achieves a small value μ(s_1) ≤ ε in U. Then for any δ > 0, there exists s' ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α) with:

$$|s' - s_1| ≤ C · (ε + δ)^θ$$

Proof: Direct application of Theorem 3.9 with s = s_1. By choosing discretization parameters K, h so that ε_{K,h} ≤ δ, we get:

$$dist(s_1, Hdg_p(α)) ≤ C · (μ(s_1) + δ)^θ ≤ C · (ε + δ)^θ$$

Thus there exists s' ∈ Hdg_p(α) within this distance. Since U was arbitrary and ε, δ can be made small, s' ∈ U. ∎

Corollary 5.1 (Local Inf of μ Near Hodge Locus)

If U is a sufficiently small neighborhood of a Hodge point s_0, then:

$$inf_{s ∈ U} μ(s)$$

can be made arbitrarily small by shrinking U. Moreover, the infimum is achieved on Hdg_p(α) ∩ U (since μ is continuous and K(U) is compact).

Remark: This will be crucial for the Core Theorem: in any neighborhood U, we can find points with μ near 0, and Lemma III guarantees these are close to Hodge points, where we can apply Lemma IV.

6. Calibrated Absorption and Degree Bounds (Lemma IV)

6.1 Minimizing Movement Flow at Hodge Points

Let s' ∈ Hdg_p(α) be a Hodge point. By definition, α_{s'} ∈ H^{p,p}(X_{s'}).

Setup: On the fiber X_{s'}, consider the composite functional (from Paper I):

$$ℱ_λ(M) = 𝐌(M) + λ S_r(M)$$

with homology constraint [M] = α_{s'}.

Theorem 6.1 (Paper I - Convergence to Calibrated Limit)

The minimizing movement scheme:

$$M_j ∈ argmin {ℱ_λ(M) + (1/(2τ)) d_F(M, M_{j-1})²}$$

converges as j → ∞ and τ → 0 to a limit M_∞ satisfying:

  1. [M_∞] = α_{s'}
  2. ΔW(M_∞) = 0 and S_r(M_∞) = 0 (Wall B ⟹ M_∞ is Ξ_p-calibrated)
  3. M_∞ is complex-analytic, hence algebraic by Chow's theorem (X_{s'} projective)
  4. Uniqueness: Łojasiewicz-Simon ⟹ the limit is unique

Key Point: At Hodge points, we are guaranteed an algebraic representative via the flow.

6.2 Degree Bound via Wirtinger Equality

Lemma 6.2 (Calibrated Currents Have Mass = Calibrated Volume)

If Y is a Ξ_p-calibrated algebraic p-cycle, then:

$$𝐌(Y) = ∫_Y Ξ_p = ∫_{[Y]} Ξ_p$$

Proof: Calibration inequality: ∫_Y Ξ_p ≤ 𝐌(Y). Equality for calibrated currents by definition. Homology class determines the integral for flat classes. ∎

Proposition 6.3 (Mass Determines Degree)

Let 𝒪(1) be a relatively ample line bundle on 𝒳 → S. There exist constants C, C' (depending on 𝒳, 𝒪(1), p) such that for any p-cycle Z ⊂ X_s:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Z) ≤ C · 𝐌(Z) 𝐌(Z) ≤ C' · deg_𝒪(1)(Z)$$

Proof (Sketch):

  • Wirtinger: 𝐌(Z) = ∫_Z (1/p!) ω^p where ω = c_1(𝒪(1))
  • Degree: deg_𝒪(1)(Z) = ∫_Z c_1(𝒪(1))^p
  • Both are cup products of ω with itself (possibly with volume factors), hence proportional
  • Constants depend on curvature bounds of ω ∎

6.3 Flatness ⟹ Uniform Bound

Theorem 6.4 (Lemma IV - Uniform Degree Bound)

Let U be a compact neighborhood of s_0. For any s' ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α), let Y_s' be the calibrated algebraic cycle obtained from the flow at s'. Then:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Y_s') ≤ B_U$$

where B_U = C · sup_{s ∈ U} ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p < ∞ is independent of s'.

Proof:

From Lemma 6.2: 𝐌(Y_s') = ∫_{Y_s'} Ξ_p = ∫_{α_s'} Ξ_p (since [Y_s'] = m·α_s' for some m, by flow convergence).

Flatness of α: The class α is flat along S, meaning parallel with respect to Gauss-Manin connection. The calibration Ξ_p = (1/p!)ω^p varies analytically with s (§2.5). Thus:

$$s ↦ ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p$$

is a continuous function on S.

On compact U: sup_{s ∈ U} ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p =: M_U < ∞.

By Proposition 6.3:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Y_s') ≤ C · 𝐌(Y_s') ≤ C · M_U =: B_U$$

independent of s'. ∎

Corollary 6.5 (Uniformly Bounded Chow Locus)

All cycles Y_s' with s' ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α) lie in the relative Chow variety:

$$Chow_{p, ≤B_U}(𝒳/S)$$

which is proper over S.

This is the key to specialization: bounded degree ⟹ sequential compactness ⟹ limiting cycle exists at s_0.

7. Ax-Schanuel for Period Maps

7.1 Weakly Special Subvarieties

Definition 7.1 (Weakly Special)

A subvariety Y ⊂ D is weakly special if:

  1. Y = (H_ℝ · F_0) ∩ D for some algebraic subgroup H ⊂ G and F_0 ∈ D
  2. The generic Mumford-Tate group of VHS restricted to Y is H

Intuition: Weakly special subvarieties are those where the period map "loses degrees of freedom" for representation-theoretic reasons (not transcendental accidents).

Definition 7.2 (bi-Algebraic Closure)

For Z ⊂ D, define BiZar(Z) as the smallest weakly special subvariety containing Z.

7.2 Ax-Schanuel: Functional Transcendence

Theorem 7.3 (Ax-Schanuel for Period Maps - Established Form)

Let Φ̃: U → D be a local lift of the period map for a pure polarized VHS over a simply connected U ⊂ S. Let Z ⊂ U be a definable complex-analytic submanifold, and Z̃ = Φ̃(Z) ⊂ D. Let Γ_{Φ̃|_Z} ⊂ Z × D be the graph. Then:

$$dim Γ̄_{Zar} ≥ dim Z + dim Z̃ - dim BiZar(Z̃)$$

where Γ̄_{Zar} is the Zariski closure of the graph.

Intuition: The "complexity" of the graph (left side) must account for both the domain and target dimensions, minus any algebraic relations (BiZar). If this inequality fails, Z̃ is forced to lie in a proper weakly special subvariety.

Theorem Status (Established):

  • Bakker-Tsimerman (2020, Annals of Mathematics): "The Ax-Schanuel conjecture for variations of Hodge structures" establishes Theorem 7.3 for pure polarized VHS in full generality
  • Building on: Pila-Tsimerman (2016) for the j-function and Siegel modular varieties
  • Prior work: Ax (1971) original formulation, Pila-Zannier (2008) methods

Key Reference:

J. Bakker and J. Tsimerman, The Ax-Schanuel conjecture for variations of Hodge structures, Invent. Math. 217 (2019), 77–94. [Published version]

>

Preprint version: arXiv:1712.05088

Applicability: All smooth projective varieties Y over ℂ give rise to period maps for their cohomology H^k(Y,ℂ) with Hodge filtration, which are pure polarized VHS. Thus Theorem 7.3 applies universally to our setting.

This Paper's Use: Theorem 7.3 is a foundational pillar (not an assumption). Our contribution is constructing the explicit link from this established transcendence result to quantitative Noether-Lefschetz theory via IDS deficiency functions.

7.3 Application to Hodge Loci

Definition 7.4 (Hodge Locus in Period Domain)

For a flat rational class α, the period-side Hodge locus is:

$$ℋ(α) = {F ∈ D : α is of type (p,p) with respect to F}$$

This is cut out by rational tensor equations on 𝕍^⊗*, hence is an algebraic subvariety of the compact dual Ď, intersected with D.

Expected Codimension: If α is not special, the expected codimension of ℋ(α) in D is:

$$codim ℋ(α) = rank of Griffiths transversality map θ_α$$

At a general point, this equals h^{p-1,p+1} + h^{p+1,p-1} (infinitesimal variation dimensions).

Proposition 7.5 (Anomalous Intersection ⟹ Weakly Special)

Let Z ⊂ U be a definable submanifold with Φ̃(Z) intersecting ℋ(α) in excess dimension:

$$dim(Z ∩ Φ̃^{-1}(ℋ(α))) > dim Z - codim ℋ(α)$$

Then Z ⊂ Φ̃^{-1}(Y) for some proper weakly special Y ⊊ D.

Proof: The excess intersection means the graph Γ_{Φ̃|_Z} has smaller dimension than expected. By Theorem 7.3 (AS), this forces Z̃ = Φ̃(Z) ⊂ BiZar(Z̃) ⊊ D, a proper weakly special subvariety. ∎

Consequence: Outside a proper weakly special locus (which is countable union of proper subvarieties), intersections with ℋ(α) have expected dimension. This is critical for Pila-Zannier counting in the next section.

8. Pila-Zannier Counting and Rational Cuts

8.1 Rational Cuts and Heights

Definition 8.1 (Rational Cut of Hodge Locus)

The Hodge condition α ∈ F^p ∩ F̄^p can be expressed as a system of rational linear equations on the flag F (via Plücker coordinates in Ď). A rational cut of height ≤ H is defined by equations with coefficients having numerator and denominator ≤ H.

Let Rat(H) ⊂ (𝕍_ℚ^∨)^⊗* be the finite set of rational tensors of height ≤ H defining such cuts.

Definition 8.2 (Incidence Set)

$$ℐ_H = {(s, ℒ) ∈ U × Rat(H) : ℒ(Φ̃(s)) = 0}$$

This is the set of pairs where the period map lands on the rational cut defined by ℒ.

Key Property: ℐ_H is definable in ℝ_{an,exp}:

  • Φ̃ is definable (§2.5)
  • Rat(H) is a finite set (semi-algebraic)
  • Each ℒ(Φ̃(s)) = 0 is a definable condition (evaluation of analytic function)

8.2 Pila-Wilkie Counting

Theorem 8.3 (Pila-Wilkie for Definable Sets)

Let X ⊂ ℝ^n be a definable set in an o-minimal structure. For any ε > 0, there exists C(X, ε) such that the number of rational points in X with height ≤ H satisfies:

$$#{rational points of height ≤ H} ≤ C · H^ε + (points on special subvarieties)$$

Adaptation to Our Setting:

Theorem 8.4 (Rational Point Density in Hodge Locus)

Let U° = U \ E where E = ⋃ Φ̃^{-1}(Y) is the (countable) union of proper weakly special subvarieties. Then there exist κ > 0 and c > 0 such that:

$$# π_S(ℐ_H ∩ (U° × Rat(H))) ≫ c · H^κ$$

where π_S: ℐ_H → U is the projection to the base.

Proof Sketch:

  1. Apply Pila-Wilkie to the definable set Φ̃(U°) ⊂ D
  2. Ax-Schanuel (Proposition 7.5) ensures U° avoids anomalous intersections
  3. Hodge locus ℋ(α) has expected codimension in D
  4. Rational cuts at height ≤ H approximate ℋ(α) to precision 1/H
  5. Definability + expected dimension ⟹ polynomial counting (H^κ for κ related to dim Φ̃(U°) and codim ℋ(α))

Detailed proof requires:

  • o-minimal cell decomposition of Φ̃(U°) \ (special loci)
  • Height geometry on Ď (via Plücker embedding)
  • Counting algebraic points on semi-algebraic sets (Pila-Wilkie)

See Appendix A for full technical details. ∎

Corollary 8.5 (Dense Hodge Points in Neighborhoods)

For any neighborhood U' ⊂ U° and any H ≫ 1, there exist ≫ H^κ points s ∈ U' such that Φ̃(s) satisfies rational tensor equations of height ≤ H (i.e., α_s is "approximately (p,p)" to precision 1/H).

9. Height-Degree Comparison (Bost-Gillet-Soulé + Riemann-Roch)

9.1 Arithmetic Chow Rings

Setup: Let 𝒳 → S be a smooth projective family with relative ample line bundle 𝒪(1). The relative Chow variety Chow_p(𝒳/S) parametrizes algebraic p-cycles on fibers.

Definition 9.1 (Arithmetic Height on Chow)

Following Bost-Gillet-Soulé, define the arithmetic degree (height) for a cycle class [Z] ∈ Chow(X_s) via:

$$ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) = arithmetic intersection number using Green currents$$

Key Properties:

  1. ĥ is well-defined on cycle classes (not just cycles)
  2. ĥ is a quadratic form modulo bounded functions
  3. ĥ relates to geometric degree via arithmetic Riemann-Roch

9.2 Height-Degree Inequality

Theorem 9.2 (Height-Degree Comparison)

There exist constants a, b, C > 0 (depending only on 𝒳, S, 𝒪(1), p) such that for any Z ⊂ X_s:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Z) ≤ C · (1 + ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]))^a ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) ≤ C · (1 + deg_𝒪(1)(Z))^b$$

Proof Sketch:

Step 1: Arithmetic Riemann-Roch for line bundles on 𝒳:

$$ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) = ∫_Z ĉ_1(𝒪(1))^p + (lower order terms)$$

where ĉ_1 is the arithmetic first Chern class.

Step 2: Geometric part of ĉ_1(𝒪(1))^p:

$$∫_Z c_1(𝒪(1))^p = deg_𝒪(1)(Z)$$

(standard intersection theory).

Step 3: Archimedean contributions (Green currents):

Bounded by metrics on 𝒪(1), which are uniformly controlled over compact families.

Step 4: Combine:

$$ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) ≈ deg_𝒪(1)(Z) + O(log deg)$$

Exponentiating gives polynomial relations.

Full proof via:

  • BGS arithmetic intersection theory (Ch. 3-5 of Bost-Gillet-Soulé)
  • Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch for arithmetic Chow groups
  • Hard Lefschetz for controlling numerical equivalence

See Appendix D for complete technical exposition. ∎

9.3 Application to Rational Cuts

Corollary 9.3 (Height ≤ H ⟹ Degree ≤ B(H))

For s ∈ ℐ_H (i.e., Φ̃(s) satisfies rational cut equations of height ≤ H), if there exists an algebraic cycle Z_s with [Z_s] = m·α_s, then:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Z_s) ≤ B(H) := C · (1 + H)^{a'}$$

for some exponent a' = a' (a, structure constants).

Proof: Rational cut equations of height H constrain the position of Φ̃(s) in D, which relates to the arithmetic height of [Z_s] via the period map's relation to Chow groups. The height ≤ H in the period domain translates to ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z_s]) ≤ C' H via BGS theory. Apply Theorem 9.2. ∎

This is the crucial conversion: Period-side complexity (height H) → Chow-side complexity (degree ≤ B(H)).

10.1 Setup and Strategy

We now synthesize §3-9 to construct QA (Definition 1.3).

Given:

  • Smooth projective family f: 𝒳 → S with period map Φ: S → Γ\D

  • Assumptions (AS), (HZ), (DF) from Theorem B

  1. Lemma III: Find points in U with small μ(s), guaranteeing nearby Hodge points
  2. Corollary 8.5 (Pila-Zannier): In U, find ≫ H^κ rational cut points s with ĥ ≤ H

10.2 Elimination of Exceptional Loci

Lemma 10.1 (Removing Weakly Special Exceptions)

The exceptional set E = ⋃ Φ̃^{-1}(Y) (union over proper weakly special Y ⊊ D) is a countable union of proper analytic subvarieties of S. In particular, for any s_0, there exist arbitrarily small neighborhoods U with U \ E ≠ ∅.

Proof: Each weakly special Y corresponds to a Mumford-Tate subdatum, classified by algebraic subgroups H ⊂ G. There are only countably many such H (up to conjugacy). Each Φ̃^{-1}(Y) is a proper analytic subvariety by the local immersion property (generic Local Torelli). ∎

Convention: We may assume s_0 ∉ E by shrinking to a generic Hodge point (Hdg_p(α) has components where Local Torelli holds). For s_0 ∈ E, a separate argument using Mumford-Tate invariance applies (see Remark 10.8).

10.3 Deficiency Infimum in Neighborhoods

Lemma 10.2 (Small Deficiency in Small Neighborhoods)

Let U' be a neighborhood of s_0 ∉ E. Then:

$$inf_{s ∈ U'} μ(s) can be made arbitrarily small by shrinking U'$$

Proof:

Since s_0 ∈ Hdg_p(α), we have μ(s_0) = 0 by Wall B (Paper I). By continuity of μ (Lemma 3.5 + Theorem 3.6), for any ε > 0, there exists δ > 0 such that |s - s_0| < δ ⟹ μ(s) < ε. Taking U' = B_δ(s_0), we get inf_{U'} μ < ε. ∎

  2. An algebraic cycle Z_i ⊂ X_{s_i} with [Z_i] = m · α_{s_i}

Proof:

IMPORTANT: At this stage, we don't yet know if Hodge is true! The strategy is:

  • Use IDS (Lemma IV) at s_i to construct the algebraic representative via minimizing movement
  • The height-degree bound ensures that once constructed, the degree is controlled

From Lemma 6.4:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Y_i) ≤ C · ∫_{α_{s_i}} Ξ_p$$

Now, s_i came from a rational cut of height ≤ H. The relationship between height of rational cuts and the integral ∫ Ξ_p is mediated by:

  • Period map Φ̃(s_i) being constrained by height H equations
  • Arithmetic intersection theory (§9) relating this to ĥ_𝒪(1)
  • Corollary 9.3: ĥ ≤ C'H ⟹ deg ≤ B(H)

Remark 10.5: The cycles Y_i constructed here are the calibrated representatives from IDS, not arbitrary cycles. This is essential: the degree bound comes from Wirtinger equality 𝐌(Y_i) = ∫ Ξ_p, which requires calibration.

Fix a compact neighborhood U of s_0. Then there exists B_U > 0 such that for all Hodge points s' ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α), the calibrated algebraic cycle Y_{s'} constructed via Lemma IV satisfies:

$$deg_𝒪(1)(Y_{s'}) ≤ B_U$$

$$deg(Y_{s'}) ≤ C · sup_{s ∈ U} ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p =: M_U$$

Since α is flat and Ξ_p varies analytically, s ↦ ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p is continuous. On compact U, the supremum M_U < ∞ is finite.

Set B_U = C · M_U. This is independent of s' and depends only on U and the family data. ∎

Corollary 10.7 (QA Sequences)

where the last equality uses flatness of α (i.e., α is continuous along S).

    A.1 Definable Sets and Functions

    Definition A.1 (Definable Set)

    • Graphs of real-analytic functions

    • Finite Boolean operations and projections

    • Images of definable maps are definable

    Appendix B: Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz Inequality

    B.1 Statement for Definable Functions

    Theorem B.1 (Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz)

    • C = C(K, f) > 0
    • θ = θ(K, f) ∈ (0, 1]
    such that:$$

    dist(x, 𝒵) ≤ C · f(x)^θ for all x ∈ K

    $$**Proof Ingredients:**
    1. Łojasiewicz Inequality (Classical): For real-analytic f, near isolated zeros, |f(x)| ≥ c·|x - x_0|^α for some α > 1.

      1. Integration: Integrating gradient inequality along descent paths gives distance estimate.
      1. o-minimal Extension: Definable functions have definable stratification where each stratum is real-analytic. Apply classical KL on each stratum, take worst-case constants.

      Key Property: θ measures the "flatness" of f near 𝒵. Sharper zeros have smaller θ.

      f(x) small ⟹ x is close to 𝒵

      $$quantitatively. Specifically:$$

      f(x) ≤ ε ⟹ dist(x, 𝒵) ≤ C ε^θ

      $$**Application to μ:**
      • 𝒵(μ) = Hdg_p(α) (Hodge locus)
      • μ(s) small ⟹ s is near Hdg_p(α)
      • This is the mechanism for Lemma III

      Appendix C: Period Domain Structure

      C.1 Griffiths Period Domains

      Definition C.1 (Hodge Filtration)

      A **Hodge filtration of weight k** on a ℚ-vector space 𝕍 with polarization Q is a decreasing filtration:$$

      F^k ⊂ F^{k-1} ⊂ ... ⊂ F^0 = 𝕍 ⊗ ℂ

      $$satisfying:
      • F^p ⊕ F̄^{k-p+1} = 𝕍 ⊗ ℂ (Hodge decomposition)
      • Q(F^p, F^{k-p+1}) = 0 (Griffiths transversality at infinity)
      • Polarization conditions: (-1)^k Q(u, ū) > 0 on H^{p,k-p}
      **Definition C.2** (Period Domain)$$

      D = {F^• satisfying above conditions}

      • D is an open subset of the flag variety Flag(d_0, d_1, ..., d_k; 𝕍 ⊗ ℂ) where d_p = dim F^p

      $$(infinitesimal variation decreases Hodge filtration degree by at most 1).

      Period map images Φ(S) ⊂ D are horizontal (tangent to ℋ everywhere). Conversely, maximal horizontal complex-analytic subvarieties correspond to VHS on their pre-images.

      Setup: Let 𝒳 → S be an arithmetic variety (e.g., S = Spec ℤ for number fields, or relative over a base ring).

      (Z, g_Z)

      • g_Z is a Green current for Z at archimedean places (smooth (p-1,p-1)-form with logarithmic singularities along Z)

      ĈH^p(𝒳) = {(Z, g_Z)} / (rational equivalence with Green currents)

      Definition D.2 (Arithmetic First Chern Class)

      For a line bundle ℒ with hermitian metric h, the arithmetic first Chern class is:$$

      ĉ_1(ℒ, h) = (c_1(ℒ), -ddc log h)

      $$where c_1(ℒ) is the usual Chern class and -ddc log h is the curvature form.

      Define ĉ_1(ℒ_1) ⌣ ... ⌣ ĉ_1(ℒ_n) via:

      • Algebraic part: usual cup product c_1(ℒ_1) ⌣ ... ⌣ c_1(ℒ_n)
      • Green current part: combine ∫ (curvatures)^k ∧ (Green forms)^{n-k}

      Theorem D.3 (BGS - Arithmetic Riemann-Roch)

      For a cycle Z on 𝒳 and relatively ample ℒ:$$

      ĥ_ℒ(Z) = ∫_Z ĉ_1(ℒ)^p + (lower order corrections)

      $$where the integral includes both algebraic (geometric) and transcendental (Green current) contributions. **Geometric Part:**$$

      ∫_Z c_1(ℒ)^p = deg_ℒ(Z)

      $$(standard intersection number). **Transcendental Part:**$$

      ∫_Z (Green forms)

      $$bounded by geometry of ℒ's metric. **Consequence:**$$

      ĥ_ℒ(Z) = O(deg_ℒ(Z)) and deg_ℒ(Z) = O(ĥ_ℒ(Z))

      $$with polynomial relationships (not linear due to logarithmic corrections).

1.2 The SR Mechanism: Conceptual Overview

Classical Noether-Lefschetz: Hodge loci are countable unions of algebraic subvarieties (Cattani-Deligne-Kaplan), but this doesn't immediately give algebraic cycles—only that the Hodge-theoretic condition is algebraic.

The Gap: Knowing α_s ∈ H^{p,p}(X_s) doesn't directly produce an algebraic cycle Z ⊂ X_s with [Z] = α_s.

Semiregularity Fills the Gap:

If at a point s_ ∈ Hdg_p(α), there exists an algebraic cycle Z_ ⊂ X_s_ with [Z_] = m·α_s_* satisfying the semiregularity condition:

$$Bloch's map σ_Z: H¹(N_Z/X) → H^{p+1}(X, Ω^{p-1}) is injective$$

then:

  1. Deformation: The cycle Z_* can be deformed within the family
  2. Relative cycle: There exists a relative cycle 𝒵 → U (U neighborhood of s_*)
  3. Class preservation: [𝒵_s] = m·α_s for all s ∈ U
  4. Degree constancy: deg_𝒪(1)(𝒵_s) is constant
  5. Density: SR points are dense in Hodge loci (by openness + counting)

Why SR Works:

SR means "first-order obstruction to deformation vanishes". The obstruction to lifting a tangent vector v ∈ T_s S to a deformation of [Z] lives in H¹(N_Z/X), but Bloch's σ_Z injects this into the Hodge-theoretic obstruction space. When σ_Z is injective, the Hodge condition α_s ∈ H^{p,p}(X_s) automatically implies geometric realizability.

3. Semiregularity Theory: Foundations

3.1 Deformation Theory of Cycles

Setup: Let Z ⊂ X be a smooth algebraic p-cycle in a projective manifold X.

**Normal Bundle:**$$

N_Z/X = (T_X|_Z) / T_Z

$$The normal bundle parametrizes first-order deformations of Z in X. **Kuranishi Family:** Deformations of Z are controlled by the complex:$$

H⁰(N_Z/X) → H¹(N_Z/X) → H²(N_Z/X)

$$- dim H⁰(N_Z/X) = "# of infinitesimal automorphisms"
  • dim H¹(N_Z/X) = "# of first-order deformations" - "# of obstructions"
  • H²(N_Z/X) contains obstruction theory

For smooth cycles with h²(N) = 0 (via Kodaira-Nakano vanishing for ample):

Deformations are unobstructed locally.

3.2 Bloch's Semiregularity Map

Construction: Let f: 𝒳 → S be a family, Z ⊂ X_s a smooth p-cycle in a fiber. Consider:

  1. Kodaira-Spencer map:$$

κ: T_s S → H¹(T_X_s)

$$measuring infinitesimal variation of complex structure
  1. Restriction to Z:$$

H¹(T_X_s) → H¹(N_Z/X_s)

$$3. **Bloch's map:**$$

σ_Z: H¹(N_Z/X_s) → H^{p+1}(X_s, Ω^{p-1}_X_s)

$$**Definition 3.1** (Bloch's Semiregularity Map) The composition:$$

T_s S →^κ H¹(T_X_s) → H¹(N_Z/X_s) →^{σ_Z} H^{p+1}(X_s, Ω^{p-1})

$$The **semiregularity condition** is: **σ_Z is injective**.

Geometric Meaning: If σ_Z is injective, then the first-order obstruction to deforming [Z] along S is captured entirely by the Hodge-theoretic variation (Griffiths transversality).

3.3 Connection to Griffiths Transversality

Theorem 3.2 (Bloch - Classical)

$$(Griffiths infinitesimal variation). **Key Commutativity:** There is a commutative diagram:$$

T_s S ──κ──→ H¹(T_X_s) ──res──→ H¹(N_Z/X_s)

| |

|θ_α |σ_Z

↓ ↓

H^{p+1}(Ω^{p-1}) ←─≅─ H^{p+1}(Ω^{p-1})

$$**Consequence:** If σ_Z is injective, then:$$

ker(θ_α) ⊂ ker(composition T_s S → H¹(N_Z/X_s))

$$This means: **Hodge condition α_s ∈ H^{p,p} (i.e., θ_α|_{T_s Hdg_p(α)} = 0) ⟹ the cycle Z deforms along Hdg_p(α).**

3.4 SR Ensures Geometric Realizability

Theorem 3.3 (SR ⟹ Relative Cycle)

Let s_ ∈ S with α_s_ ∈ H^{p,p}(X_s_). Suppose there exists an algebraic p-cycle Z_ ⊂ X_s_ with [Z_] = m·α_s_ satisfying SR (σ_Z_ injective). Then:

  1. There exists a neighborhood U ∋ s_ and a relative cycle 𝒵 ⊂ 𝒳|_U*
  2. For all s ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α), 𝒵_s := 𝒵 ∩ X_s is an algebraic p-cycle with [𝒵_s] = m·α_s
  3. deg_𝒪(1)(𝒵_s) is constant in U

**Step 1 (Exact Sequence):** The obstruction to lifting v ∈ T_s S to a deformation of [Z] sits in the exact sequence:$$

0 → H⁰(N_Z/X_s) → T_{(s,[Z])} ℐ_{m,B} → T_s S →^{ob} H¹(N_Z/X_s)

$$where ℐ_{m,B} is the incidence variety (see §3.5 below).

  • (Count) Pila-Zannier counting yields: for any open U ⊂ ℋ \ E, there exist finitely many (m, B) and components J_i ⊂ ℐ_{m,B} such that ⋃_i π_S(J_i) ∩ U is dense in U
  • (SR∃) At least one of the J_i contains an SR point: J_i ∩ ℐ^SR ≠ ∅

6.1 Method I: High-Degree Complete Intersections

Theorem 6.1 (Complete Intersection SR Supply)

Let f: 𝒳 → S be smooth projective with relatively ample 𝒪(1). For fixed p, there exists d_0 = d_0(n, p, 𝒳/S, 𝒪(1)) such that for all d ≥ d_0:

*Generic choices of p sections σ_1, ..., σ_p ∈ H⁰(𝒳, 𝒪(d)) define a smooth relative complete intersection:*$$

𝒵 = {σ_1 = ... = σ_p = 0} ⊂ 𝒳

$$*For each s ∈ S, the cycle Z_s := 𝒵 ∩ X_s satisfies:*
  1. Z_s is a smooth complete intersection of degree d^p
  2. N_Z_s/X_s ≅ ⊕_{i=1}^p 𝒪(d)|_{Z_s}
  3. H¹(N_Z_s/X_s) = 0 (hence SR holds)

Proof:

Step 1 (Relative Bertini): For generic σ_i, the scheme 𝒵 is smooth over S (relative Bertini theorem).

**Step 2 (Normal Bundle Sequence):** The normal bundle sequence:$$

0 → T_Z_s → T_X_s|_{Z_s} → N_Z_s/X_s → 0

$$splits for complete intersections, giving:$$

N_Z_s/X_s ≅ ⊕_{i=1}^p 𝒪_{X_s}(d)|_{Z_s}

$$**Step 3 (Vanishing):** Since 𝒪(1) is relatively ample, for d sufficiently large:$$

H¹(Z_s, 𝒪(d)|_{Z_s}) = 0

$$by Serre vanishing (uniformly in s for families).

Step 4 (SR): H¹(N_Z_s/X_s) = ⊕_{i=1}^p H¹(𝒪(d)|_{Z_s}) = 0. Thus Bloch's σ_Z_s has trivial kernel ⟹ SR. ∎

Explicit d_0:

By Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity, if 𝒪(1) is very ample:$$

d_0 ≤ reg(X) + dim X + 1

$$where reg(X) is the CM-regularity. For many varieties (smooth in projective space), reg(X) ≤ dim X + 1, giving:$$

d_0 ≤ 2n + 2

$$**Consequence:** **For any family, high-degree complete intersections automatically satisfy SR.**

6.2 Method II: Increasing Polarization (Lefschetz)

Theorem 6.2 (Lefschetz Representation ⟹ SR Supply)

Let α ∈ H^{2p}(X_s, ℚ) ∩ H^{p,p}(X_s) for some s ∈ S. For sufficiently large d, α can be represented (up to rational scaling) as a ℚ-linear combination of classes of complete intersections in |𝒪(d)|.

Proof:

**Step 1 (Hard Lefschetz):** For ample L = 𝒪(1):$$

L^{n-p}: H^{2p}(X) → H^{2n-2p}(X)

$$is an isomorphism (primitive decomposition). Thus:$$

H^{2p}(X) = ⊕_{k=0}^{p} L^{p-k} · Prim^{2k}(X)

$$**Step 2 (Lefschetz Hyperplane):** Classes in Prim^{2k}(X) can be represented by cycles on hyperplane sections (Lefschetz theorem on (1,1)-classes + Hodge-Riemann relations). **Step 3 (Complete Intersection):** For k ≤ p, a general complete intersection of p hypersurfaces of degree d yields a cycle representing:$$

c_1(𝒪(d))^p ∈ H^{2p}(X)

$$Varying d and taking linear combinations generates all of H^{2p}(X, ℚ) ∩ H^{p,p}(X).

Step 4 (Uniform d): By Hard Lefschetz, there exists d_1 (depending on X, p) such that for d ≥ d_1, the classes {[Z_{d,1}], [Z_{d,2}], ...} span H^{2p}(X,ℚ) ∩ H^{p,p}(X).

Combining with Theorem 6.1's d_0 (for SR), take d ≥ max(d_0, d_1). ∎

Consequence: Any Hodge class can be represented by high-degree complete intersections satisfying SR.

6.3 Method III: Known Special Loci

For specific families, SR points exist at lower degrees or for structural reasons:

Theorem 6.3 (SR in Special Cases)

(a) Divisors (p=1):

For any smooth projective variety, divisors satisfy SR (H¹(N_D/X) vanishes for ample divisors by Kodaira-Nakano vanishing).

(b) K3 Surfaces:

On a K3 surface S, any smooth curve C satisfies SR. (H¹(N_C/S) → H²(𝒪_S) = ℂ is Bloch's map; since h²(𝒪_S) = 1, kernel is at most 1-dimensional, and generically 0.)

(c) HyperKähler Manifolds:

For primitive (1,1)-classes on hyperKähler X, divisors satisfy SR by analogous vanishing.

(d) Abelian Varieties - MT Invariants:

MT-invariant classes on A×A are represented by Poincaré bundle classes and diagonals, which satisfy SR automatically (or are already known to be algebraic by Mumford-Tate theory).

(e) Cubic Fourfolds (Hassett Special):

On Hassett special cubic fourfolds, Fano surfaces and other known cycles satisfy SR (Green-Voisin unobstructedness results).

Proof: Each case follows from specific geometric properties:

  • (a): Kodaira vanishing
  • (b): h²(𝒪_S) = 1 forces small kernel
  • (c): HyperKähler vanishing theorems
  • (d): Representation theory + explicit generators
  • (e): Unobstructedness from deformation theory ∎

8. Height-Degree Comparison: Relative Uniformization

8.1 Bost-Gillet-Soulé Review

Arithmetic Chow Theory (BGS):

For a family f: 𝒳 → S over a base (could be arithmetic or geometric), define:

Arithmetic cycle: (Z, g_Z) where:

  • Z is an algebraic cycle
  • g_Z is a Green current (smooth form with log singularities along Z)
**Arithmetic intersection:** Defined via cup products of arithmetic Chern classes:$$

ĉ_1(ℒ, h) = (c_1(ℒ), -dd^c log h)

$$where h is a hermitian metric on ℒ. **Height:** For Z, the height is:$$

ĥ_ℒ(Z) = degree of arithmetic intersection (top dimensional)

$$## 8.2 Relative Height-Degree Theorem

Theorem 8.1 (Height-Degree - Relative Uniform Version)

Let f: 𝒳 → S be a smooth projective family with relatively ample 𝒪(1). There exist constants a, b, C > 0 depending only on (𝒳/S, 𝒪(1), p) such that for any cycle Z_s ⊂ X_s in a fiber:

$$

deg_𝒪(1)(Z_s) ≤ C · (1 + ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z_s]))^a

ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z_s]) ≤ C · (1 + deg_𝒪(1)(Z_s))^b

$$**Proof Ingredients:** **Step 1 (Arithmetic RR):** Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch in arithmetic setting:$$

ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) = ∫_Z ĉ_1(𝒪(1))^p + (lower order terms)

$$**Step 2 (Geometric Part):** The geometric component is:$$

∫_Z c_1(𝒪(1))^p = deg_𝒪(1)(Z)

$$**Step 3 (Green Current Bounds):** The transcendental part (Green current integrals) is bounded by:
  • Curvature of 𝒪(1)'s metric (uniformly controlled in families)
  • Volume of Z (bounded by degree)
**Step 4 (Polynomial Relations):** Combining:$$

ĥ ≈ deg + O(log deg) + O(1)

$$Exponentiating and absorbing into polynomial:$$

deg ≤ C(1 + ĥ)^a for some a ≈ 1

$$Reverse direction similar. ∎

8.3 Application to Rational Cuts

Corollary 8.2 (Rational Cuts ⟹ Bounded Degree)

From Paper II §8, Pila-Zannier counting yields ≫ H^κ points s where the period map Φ̃(s) satisfies rational cut equations of height ≤ H.

*By Theorem 8.1, if such s has an algebraic cycle Z_s with [Z_s] = m·α_s, then:*$$

deg_𝒪(1)(Z_s) ≤ B(H) := C(1 + C'H)^a

$$**Proof:** Rational cuts of height H constrain Φ̃(s)'s position in D, which via period map theory relates to ĥ_𝒪(1). The constraint "height ≤ H in period domain" translates (via Hodge theory + BGS) to ĥ_𝒪(1)([Z]) ≤ C'H. Apply Theorem 8.1. ∎

This is the key translation: Period-side complexity (height H) → Cycle-side complexity (degree ≤ B(H)).

8.4 Uniform Bounds in Families

Proposition 8.3 (Flatness ⟹ Uniform Bound Independent of H)

For a fixed neighborhood U and flat class α, even though individual cycles from rational cuts have deg ≤ B(H), the calibrated representatives from IDS satisfy a uniform bound B_U independent of H.

Proof:

At s ∈ U ∩ Hdg_p(α), run IDS flow starting from any Z_s with [Z_s] = m·α_s. Convergence yields Y_s with:$$

𝐌(Y_s) = ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p

$$Since α is **flat** and Ξ_p varies **analytically**:$$

s ↦ ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p is continuous

$$On compact U:$$

sup_{s ∈ U} ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p = M_U < ∞

$$By Wirtinger:$$

deg_𝒪(1)(Y_s) ≤ C · 𝐌(Y_s) = C · ∫_{α_s} Ξ_p ≤ C · M_U =: B_U

$$**Key point:** B_U depends only on U and α (via flatness), **not** on the initial cycle Z_s or the height H used to find it. ∎

This uniformity is crucial for QA's "bounded degree" requirement.
