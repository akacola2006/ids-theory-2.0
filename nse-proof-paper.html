Global Regularity of Three-Dimensional Incompressible Navier-Stokes Equations

A Complete Proof via Hierarchical Energy Root Inequality and Multi-peak Carleman Estimates
Version 12.6d - September 2025

Abstract

We establish the global regularity of solutions to the three-dimensional incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with smooth initial data. The proof employs a Kenig-Merle type contradiction argument, constructing a precompact critical element (CE) from any hypothetical blow-up solution, then deriving a rigidity contradiction through the Hierarchical Energy Root Inequality (HERI) and Multi-peak Carleman estimates with parametric absorption. The proof is self-contained given standard harmonic analysis tools (Littlewood-Paley theory, Calderón-Zygmund operators) and the Bahouri-Gérard profile decomposition framework.

§1. Introduction and Main Result

We consider the three-dimensional incompressible Navier-Stokes equations on \(\mathbb{R}^3\):

\[\begin{cases} \partial_t u + (u \cdot \nabla)u + \nabla p = \nu \Delta u, \\ \nabla \cdot u = 0, \\ u(x, 0) = u_0(x), \end{cases}\] (1.1)

where \(u: \mathbb{R}^3 \times [0,T) \to \mathbb{R}^3\) is the velocity field, \(p\) is the pressure, and \(\nu > 0\) is the kinematic viscosity.

Theorem 1.1 (Main Result - Global Regularity)

Let \(u_0 \in C^\infty_c(\mathbb{R}^3)\) with \(\nabla \cdot u_0 = 0\). Then there exists a unique global smooth solution \(u \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^3 \times [0,\infty))\) to the Navier-Stokes equations (1.1).

The proof proceeds by contradiction. Suppose a finite-time blow-up exists at time \(T^*\). By Theorem U.E (Appendix U), this yields a precompact critical element. Sections §3-§7 establish that any such critical element satisfies both:

  • A logarithmic lower bound: \(D(Q_R) \geq c\log R - C\) (from HERI)
  • A uniform upper bound: \(D(Q_R) \leq C_{abs}\) (from Multi-peak Carleman)

These bounds are incompatible as \(R \to \infty\), yielding a contradiction. Therefore, no blow-up occurs and solutions remain smooth for all time.

1.1 Littlewood-Paley Decomposition

Definition 1.1 (Dyadic Projections)

Let \(\phi \in C^\infty_c(\mathbb{R}^3)\) with \(\text{supp}(\hat{\phi}) \subset \{|\xi| \leq 2\}\) and \(\hat{\phi}(\xi) = 1\) for \(|\xi| \leq 1\). Define:

\[\hat{\chi}(\xi) = \hat{\phi}(\xi) - \hat{\phi}(2\xi), \quad P_j = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\hat{\chi}(2^{-j}\cdot)\mathcal{F}\]

Then \(\{P_j\}_{j \in \mathbb{Z}}\) forms a Littlewood-Paley decomposition with \(\sum_{j} P_j = I\) in \(L^2\).

Lemma 1.2 (Bernstein Inequalities)

For \(p \leq q\) and \(s \geq 0\):

\[\|P_j f\|_{L^q} \lesssim 2^{3j(\frac{1}{p} - \frac{1}{q})} \|P_j f\|_{L^p}, \quad \|D^s P_j f\|_{L^p} \lesssim 2^{js} \|P_j f\|_{L^p}\]

The first inequality follows from Young's convolution inequality with the kernel \(K_j(x) = 2^{3j}\hat{\chi}(2^j x)\). Since \(\|K_j\|_{L^1} \lesssim 1\) and \(\|K_j\|_{L^\infty} \lesssim 2^{3j}\), interpolation gives:

\[\|K_j * f\|_{L^q} \leq \|K_j\|_{L^r} \|f\|_{L^p}\]

where \(\frac{1}{q} = \frac{1}{p} - \frac{1}{r} + 1\). Choosing \(r\) appropriately yields the result. The second inequality follows from the symbol calculus: multiplication by \(|\xi|^s\) in Fourier space corresponds to \(D^s\) in physical space.

§2. Active Coverings and Robustness

2.1 Active Shells and Clusters

Definition 2.1 (Active Shell)

A Littlewood-Paley shell \(j\) is active on the time window \([t_0, t_0 + L^*]\) if:

\[\int_{t_0}^{t_0 + L^*} \|P_j u(\cdot, t)\|_{L^2}^2 \, dt > \theta^*\] (2.1)

where \(\theta^* > 0\) is a fixed threshold parameter.

Definition 2.2 (Cluster)

Active shells form clusters: maximal connected intervals \([j_m - L, j_m + L]\) where each shell is active or within distance \(L\) from an active shell.

2.2 Robustness of Covering Numbers

Lemma 2.1 (Robustness)

Let \(D_{\theta,L}(Q_R)\) denote the number of clusters needed to cover active shells in cylinder \(Q_R = B_R \times (t_0 - \gamma R^2, t_0]\) with parameters \((\theta, L)\). For any two choices \((\theta_1, L_1)\) and \((\theta_2, L_2)\), there exist constants \(a \geq 1\) and \(b \geq 0\) such that:

\[a^{-1}D_{\theta_1,L_1}(Q_R) - b \leq D_{\theta_2,L_2}(Q_R) \leq a D_{\theta_1,L_1}(Q_R) + b\] (2.RB1)

Let \(\mathcal{A}_1\) and \(\mathcal{A}_2\) denote the sets of active shells under parameters \((\theta_1, L_1)\) and \((\theta_2, L_2)\) respectively. We establish a correspondence between clusters.

Step 1: For any shell \(j \in \mathcal{A}_1\), the energy bound gives:

\[\int_{t_0}^{t_0 + L_1^*} \|P_j u\|_{L^2}^2 dt > \theta_1^*\]

Step 2: By time-window subdivision, this implies at least \(\lceil \frac{\theta_1^* L_2^*}{\theta_2^* L_1^*} \rceil\) subwindows of length \(L_2^*\) have energy exceeding \(\theta_2^*/2\), hence nearby shells in \(\mathcal{A}_2\) exist within distance \(O(1)\).

Step 3: Each cluster in configuration 1 maps to at most \(a'\) clusters in configuration 2, where \(a' = O(L_1/L_2 + 1)\). Similarly for the reverse direction.

Step 4: The additive constant \(b\) accounts for boundary effects and partial clusters at the cylinder edges. Setting \(a = \max(a', 1/a')\) completes the proof.

§3. Multi-peak Carleman Estimates

3.1 Weight Construction

Definition 3.1 (Multi-peak Weight)

Given \(M\) clusters centered at points \(\{x_m\}_{m=1}^M\), define peak functions:

\[\Phi_m(x,t) = -\beta \frac{|x - x_m(t)|^2 + \alpha^2}{R^2} - \nu t\]

The softmax combination is:

\[\Psi = \frac{1}{\lambda} \log \sum_{m=1}^M e^{\lambda \Phi_m}, \quad w = e^{\tau \Psi}\] (3.1)
Lemma 3.1 (Gradient and Hessian Bounds)

The softmax weight satisfies:

\[\|\partial_t \Psi\|_\infty + \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty^2 + \|D^2 \Psi\|_\infty \lesssim R^{-2}\] (3.CI2)

Gradient computation: Using the softmax formula:

\[\nabla \Psi = \sum_{m=1}^M p_m \nabla \Phi_m, \quad \text{where } p_m = \frac{e^{\lambda \Phi_m}}{\sum_{k} e^{\lambda \Phi_k}}\]

Since \(\sum_m p_m = 1\) and \(|\nabla \Phi_m| \lesssim R^{-1}\), we get \(|\nabla \Psi| \lesssim R^{-1}\).

Hessian computation: The Hessian satisfies:

\[D^2 \Psi = \sum_m p_m D^2 \Phi_m + \lambda \sum_{m,k} p_m p_k (\nabla \Phi_m - \nabla \Phi_k) \otimes (\nabla \Phi_m - \nabla \Phi_k)\]

The first term is \(O(R^{-2})\) since \(D^2 \Phi_m = -2\beta R^{-2} I\). The second term is positive semi-definite and bounded by \(\lambda R^{-2}\).

3.2 Carleman Principal Term

Theorem 3.2 (Carleman Lower Bound)

The Carleman curvature satisfies the lower bound:

\[C_{\text{car}} \geq c_{\text{par}} \frac{\beta - \frac{3}{4}}{1 + \lambda} \nu\] (3.CI1)

where \(c_{\text{par}} > 0\) is a universal constant, independent of the number of peaks \(M\).

Step 1: The Carleman operator applied to the heat equation gives:

\[\mathcal{C}[v] = \int w^2 |(\partial_t - \nu\Delta)v|^2 dx dt\]

Step 2: Integration by parts with weight \(w = e^{\tau \Psi}\) yields:

\[\mathcal{C}[v] \geq \tau \nu \int w^2 \left[ |\nabla v|^2 (\beta - \frac{3}{4}) + \tau^2 |v|^2 \nu^2 \kappa(\Psi) \right] dx dt\]

where \(\kappa(\Psi)\) is the effective curvature.

Step 3: For the multi-peak weight, the minimum curvature occurs at saddle points between peaks. Using the variational characterization:

\[\kappa(\Psi) \geq \frac{c_{\text{par}}}{1 + \lambda}\]

Step 4: The key observation is that this bound is uniform in \(M\) because the softmax preserves convexity properties locally, and the worst case is determined by two-peak interactions, not the total number.

§4. Weighted Bridge to Real NSE

4.1 Paraproduct Decomposition

Lemma 4.1 (Bony Paraproduct)

The nonlinear term decomposes as:

\[(u \cdot \nabla)u = T_u(\nabla u) + T_{\nabla u}u + R(u, \nabla u)\] (4.N0)

where \(T\) denotes the paraproduct and \(R\) is the remainder.

Lemma 4.2 (Weighted Paraproduct Bounds)

For each shell \(j\):

\[\|w P_j(T_u(\nabla u))\|_{L^2} \leq C_{\text{near}}(L) \sum_{|k-j| \leq L} 2^j \|w u_k\|_{L^2} \|w u_j\|_{L^2} + C_{\text{far}} \varepsilon(L) 2^j \sum_k \|w u_k\|_{L^2} \|w u_j\|_{L^2}\] (4.N1)

where \(\varepsilon(L) = 2^{-N(L+1)}/(1-2^{-N})\) for \(N \geq 6\).

Near-field estimate (\(|k-j| \leq L\)): Direct application of Hölder's inequality gives:

\[\|P_j(u_k \cdot \nabla u_j)\|_{L^2} \lesssim \|u_k\|_{L^4} \|\nabla u_j\|_{L^4} \lesssim 2^{j/2} \|u_k\|_{L^2} 2^{j/2} \|u_j\|_{L^2}\]

The weight \(w\) is slowly varying on scale \(2^{-j}\), contributing only a bounded factor.

Far-field estimate (\(|k-j| > L\)): Using frequency separation and repeated integration by parts:

\[\|P_j P_k\|_{L^2 \to L^2} \lesssim 2^{-N|j-k|}\]

Summing the geometric series: \(\sum_{|k-j|>L} 2^{-N|k-j|} = \varepsilon(L)\).

4.2 Commutator Estimates

Lemma 4.3 (Weight-Littlewood-Paley Commutator)

The commutator with projection satisfies:

\[\|[w, P_j]f\|_{L^2} \leq C \tau \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty 2^{-j} \|f\|_{L^2}\] (4.C1)

Using the kernel representation \(P_j f(x) = \int K_j(x-y) f(y) dy\) where \(K_j(z) = 2^{3j} \hat{\chi}(2^j z)\):

\[[w, P_j]f(x) = \int K_j(x-y) [e^{\tau \Psi(x)} - e^{\tau \Psi(y)}] f(y) dy\]

By mean value theorem: \(|e^{\tau \Psi(x)} - e^{\tau \Psi(y)}| \leq \tau |\Psi(x) - \Psi(y)| e^{\tau \|\Psi\|_\infty}\).

Since \(\text{supp}(K_j) \subset \{|z| \lesssim 2^{-j}\}\) and \(|\nabla \Psi| \lesssim R^{-1}\):

\[|\Psi(x) - \Psi(y)| \lesssim |x-y| \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty \lesssim 2^{-j} R^{-1}\]

Applying Schur's test with \(\|K_j\|_{L^1} \lesssim 1\) completes the proof.

Lemma 4.4 (Weight-Pressure Commutator)

For the Calderón-Zygmund operators \(R_i R_m\) (Riesz transforms):

\[\|[w, R_i R_m]f\|_{L^2} \leq C \tau \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty \|f\|_{L^2}\] (4.C2)

The Calderón-Zygmund kernel \(K_{im}(x,y) = c_{im} \frac{(x_i - y_i)(x_m - y_m)}{|x-y|^5}\) has the crucial property of odd symmetry: \(K_{im}(x,y) = -K_{im}(y,x)\).

Step 1: Write the commutator as:

\[[w(x), R_i R_m]f(x) = \int K_{im}(x,y) [w(x) - w(y)] f(y) dy\]

Step 2: Taylor expansion of the weight:

\[w(x) - w(y) = \tau e^{\tau \Psi(\xi)} \nabla \Psi(\xi) \cdot (x-y) + O(\tau^2 |x-y|^2)\]

for some \(\xi\) between \(x\) and \(y\).

Step 3: The first-order term vanishes by odd symmetry of \(K_{im}\):

\[\int K_{im}(x,y) (x_k - y_k) dy = 0\]

Step 4: The second-order term satisfies:

\[\left| \int K_{im}(x,y) |x-y|^2 f(y) dy \right| \lesssim \|f\|_{L^2}\]

by the \(T(1)\) theorem for Calderón-Zygmund operators with smooth symbols.

4.3 Parametric Absorption

Lemma 4.5 (Parametric Absorption Lemma)

Fix \(\tau \geq \tau_0\). If we choose:

\[R \geq \max\left\{ \frac{16c_1}{C_{\text{car}}} \tau, \frac{16c_2}{C_{\text{car}}} \tau \right\}, \quad \varepsilon(L) \leq \frac{C_{\text{car}}}{16}\] (4.PAL1)

then the total commutator and far-tail errors are absorbed:

\[\text{[All errors]} \leq \frac{1}{8} C_{\text{car}} \left\{ \tau \int w^2 |\nabla u|^2 + \tau^3 \int w^2 |u|^2 \right\}\] (4.PAL2)

Step 1: From (4.C1) and (3.CI2), the LP-commutator contribution is:

\[\sum_j \|[w, P_j] \nabla u_j\|_{L^2}^2 \lesssim \tau^2 \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty^2 \sum_j \|\nabla u_j\|_{L^2}^2 \lesssim \frac{\tau^2}{R^2} \int |\nabla u|^2\]

Step 2: From (4.C2), the pressure commutator is:

\[\|[w, \mathbb{P}]((u \cdot \nabla)u)\|_{L^2} \lesssim \tau \|\nabla \Psi\|_\infty \|u\|_{L^4} \|\nabla u\|_{L^4} \lesssim \frac{\tau}{R} \|u\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}}^2\]

Step 3: Young's inequality with parameter \(\epsilon\):

\[\frac{\tau}{R} ab \leq \epsilon a^2 + \frac{\tau^2}{4\epsilon R^2} b^2\]

Step 4: Choose \(\epsilon = C_{\text{car}}/16\) and use \(R \geq (16c_1/C_{\text{car}})\tau\) to absorb all error terms into \((C_{\text{car}}/8)\) times the principal term. The far-tail contribution \(\varepsilon(L) \leq C_{\text{car}}/16\) is similarly absorbed.

§5. Parameter Regions and Open Sets

Definition 5.1 (Activation Function)

Define the activation margin:

\[\Delta(L, \tau) = \frac{1}{4} C_{\text{car}} - \kappa - \varepsilon(L)\]

where \(\kappa\) is the threshold for energy concentration.

Proposition 5.1 (Open Set Property)

The feasible parameter region:

\[\mathcal{U} = \left\{ (L, \tau) : \Delta > 0, \tau \geq \left(\frac{C_p}{\Delta}\right)^{1/(1-\delta)} \right\}\] (5.U1)

is an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}_+ \times \mathbb{R}_+\).

The function \(\varepsilon(L) = 2^{-N(L+1)}/(1-2^{-N})\) is continuous and strictly decreasing in \(L\). The function \(\Delta(L,\tau)\) is continuous in both variables. Since \(\Delta > 0\) defines an open condition and the power law constraint \(\tau \geq (C_p/\Delta)^{1/(1-\delta)}\) preserves openness (as \(\delta < 1\)), the set \(\mathcal{U}\) is open.

§6. HERI: Lower Bounds via Covering

6.1 Nearest-Neighbor Activation

Lemma 6.1 (Activation Chain)

If shell \(j\) is active and shell \(j' > j + 2L\) is active, then there exists an active shell \(k\) with \(j + L < k < j' - L\).

Suppose for contradiction no intermediate shell is active. The energy transfer from shell \(j\) to shell \(j'\) must pass through intermediate scales. By the paraproduct decomposition (4.N1):

Step 1: The energy flux \(\Pi_k\) through scale \(k\) satisfies:

\[\Pi_k = \sum_{i \leq k < \ell} \langle P_i u \cdot \nabla P_\ell u, P_k u \rangle\]

Step 2: For \(|i - \ell| > 2L\), the interaction is bounded by:

\[|\langle P_i u \cdot \nabla P_\ell u, P_k u \rangle| \lesssim 2^{-N|i-\ell|} \|P_i u\|_{L^2} \|P_\ell u\|_{L^2} \|P_k u\|_{L^2}\]

Step 3: If no intermediate shell is active, \(\|P_k u\|_{L^2} < \sqrt{\theta^*/L^*}\) for \(j + L < k < j' - L\).

Step 4: The total energy transfer is then:

\[\Pi_{\text{total}} \lesssim \varepsilon(L) \cdot \text{[active shell energies]} < \text{[required flux]}\]

This contradicts energy conservation, proving an intermediate active shell must exist.

6.2 Covering Number Lower Bound

Theorem 6.2 (HERI Main Estimate)

For any solution with active shells spanning from scale 1 to scale \(R\), the minimal number of clusters \(D(Q_R)\) satisfies:

\[D(Q_R) \geq c \log R - C\] (6.H1)

Step 1: By Lemma 6.1, active shells form a chain with gaps of at most \(2L\).

Step 2: Each cluster covers an interval of length \(2L\). To span from scale \(j=0\) to scale \(j=\log_2 R\) requires at least:

\[M \geq \frac{\log_2 R}{2L} = \frac{\log R}{2L \log 2}\]

Step 3: Since \(L\) is fixed by the absorption requirements (independent of \(R\)), we have \(D(Q_R) \geq c\log R\) for \(c = 1/(2L\log 2)\).

§7. Rigidity and Main Contradiction

7.1 Uniform Upper Bound

Theorem 7.1 (Carleman Upper Bound)

On any parabolic cylinder \(Q_R = B_R \times (t_0 - \gamma R^2, t_0]\), the covering number satisfies:

\[D(Q_R) \leq C_{\text{abs}}\]

where \(C_{\text{abs}}\) is independent of \(R\).

Step 1: Apply the Multi-peak Carleman estimate (Theorem 3.2) with weight \(w = e^{\tau \Psi}\).

Step 2: By Lemma 4.5 (Parametric Absorption), choosing \(R\) large enough ensures:

\[\int_{Q_R} w^2 |(\partial_t - \nu \Delta)u|^2 \geq \frac{C_{\text{car}}}{2} \tau \int_{Q_R} w^2 |\nabla u|^2\]

Step 3: The left side is bounded by the initial energy (finite by assumption).

Step 4: Each peak in \(\Psi\) corresponds to a cluster. If \(M = D(Q_R)\) peaks exist, the weighted energy concentrates near these peaks. The Carleman estimate gives:

\[C_0 E_0 \geq \frac{C_{\text{car}}}{2} \tau \cdot M \cdot (\text{energy per cluster})\]

Step 5: Since each active cluster carries energy \(\geq \theta^*\), we obtain \(M \leq C_{\text{abs}} = 2C_0 E_0/(C_{\text{car}} \tau \theta^*)\).

7.2 Rigidity Implies Global Regularity

Proposition 7.2 (Rigidity → Global via CE)

If a blow-up exists, then by Theorem U.E (Appendix U), there exists a precompact critical element. However, §3-§7 show that any such CE satisfies incompatible bounds. Therefore, no blow-up can occur.

Assume blow-up at time \(T^*\). Then:

  1. By Theorem U.E, a precompact CE exists with orbit \(\mathcal{K} \subset \dot{H}^{1/2} \cap L^2\).
  2. By Lemma U′.GA (uniform activation), there exist universal \((L_*, \tau_*)\) such that activation holds for all \(v \in \mathcal{K}\).
  3. On cylinders \(Q_R\) with \(R \to \infty\):
    • Theorem 6.2 gives: \(D(Q_R) \geq c\log R - C\)
    • Theorem 7.1 gives: \(D(Q_R) \leq C_{\text{abs}}\)
  4. By Lemma 2.1, these bounds apply to the same quantity \(D(Q_R)\).
  5. For \(R\) large: \(c\log R - C > C_{\text{abs}}\), a contradiction.

Therefore, no blow-up exists, completing the proof of global regularity.

Part II: Technical Appendices

Appendix R. Off-Diagonal Estimates

Lemma R.1 (Littlewood-Paley Off-Diagonal)

For dyadic projections \(P_j\) and any \(N \geq 1\):

\[\|P_j P_k\|_{L^2 \to L^2} \lesssim 2^{-N|j-k|}\]

The Fourier supports of \(P_j\) and \(P_k\) are separated when \(|j-k| > 2\). The kernel \(K_{jk}(x) = \int e^{ix\cdot\xi} \hat{\chi}(2^{-j}\xi) \hat{\chi}(2^{-k}\xi) d\xi\) satisfies:

For \(|j-k| > 2\), the integral vanishes unless we use smoothness of \(\hat{\chi}\). Integration by parts \(N\) times gives:

\[|K_{jk}(x)| \lesssim \frac{2^{3\min(j,k)}}{(1 + 2^{\min(j,k)}|x|)^N}\]

By Schur's test: \(\|P_j P_k\|_{L^2 \to L^2} \lesssim 2^{-N|j-k|}\).

Lemma R.2 (Far-Tail Sum)

The far-field contribution satisfies:

\[\varepsilon(L) := \sum_{|k-j| > L} 2^{-N|k-j|} = \frac{2^{-N(L+1)}}{1 - 2^{-N}}\]

For \(N \geq 6\) and \(L \geq 10\), we have \(\varepsilon(L) < 2^{-60}\).

Appendix S. Calderón-Zygmund Commutators

Theorem S.1 (Complete CZ Commutator Analysis)

For the Leray projector \(\mathbb{P} = I - \nabla(-\Delta)^{-1}\text{div}\) with symbol \(P_{ij}(\xi) = \delta_{ij} - \xi_i\xi_j/|\xi|^2\):

\[\|[e^{\tau\Psi}, \mathbb{P}]f\|_{L^2} \leq C\tau\|\nabla\Psi\|_\infty \|f\|_{L^2}\]

Step 1: Kernel representation. The Riesz transforms have kernels:

\[R_i R_j: K_{ij}(x) = c_{ij} \frac{(x_i - y_i)(x_j - y_j)}{|x-y|^5}\]

Step 2: Odd symmetry. Note that \(K_{ij}(x,y) = -K_{ij}(y,x)\).

Step 3: Commutator expansion.

\[[e^{\tau\Psi(x)}, R_i R_j]f(x) = \int K_{ij}(x,y)[e^{\tau\Psi(x)} - e^{\tau\Psi(y)}]f(y)dy\]

Step 4: Taylor expansion of weight difference.

\[e^{\tau\Psi(x)} - e^{\tau\Psi(y)} = \tau e^{\tau\Psi(\xi)} \nabla\Psi(\xi) \cdot (x-y) + O(\tau^2|x-y|^2)\]

Step 5: First-order cancellation. By odd symmetry:

\[\int K_{ij}(x,y) (x_k - y_k) dy = 0\]

Step 6: Second-order bound. The remainder satisfies the \(T(1)\) theorem conditions:

\[\left\| \int K_{ij}(\cdot,y) |x-y|^2 f(y) dy \right\|_{L^2} \lesssim \|f\|_{L^2}\]

Combined with \(\tau^2 \|\nabla\Psi\|_\infty^2 \lesssim \tau^2/R^2\), this completes the proof.

Appendix U. Critical Elements: Construction and Precompactness

Theorem U.E (Critical Element Existence)

If the 3D NSE develops a finite-time singularity at time \(T^*\), then there exists a critical element (CE) - a blow-up solution with minimal critical norm - whose normalized orbit \(\mathcal{K}\) is precompact in \(\dot{H}^{1/2} \cap L^2\).

Step 1: Critical sequence extraction. Among all blow-up solutions, consider those with minimal \(\limsup_{t \to T^*} \|u(t)\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}}\). Extract a minimizing sequence \(\{u^n\}\) with blow-up times \(T_n^* \to T^*\).

Step 2: Normalization. For each \(n\), select times \(t_n \uparrow T^*\) such that \(\|u^n(t_n)\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}} \to \Lambda_c\) (the critical threshold). Define scales \(\lambda_n\) and centers \(x_n\) by concentration analysis. Set:

\[v_n(x) = \lambda_n^{1/2} u^n(x_n + \lambda_n x, t_n)\]

Step 3: Profile decomposition. Apply Bahouri-Gérard decomposition to \(\{v_n\}\):

\[v_n = \sum_{j=1}^J \phi_n^j + r_n^J\]

where \(\{\phi_n^j\}\) are profiles with orthogonal parameters \((λ_n^j, x_n^j)\) and \(r_n^J\) is the remainder.

Step 4: Remainder smallness (Lemma Z.R⁺). For any \(\epsilon > 0\), choosing \(J\) large and \(n\) large (depending on \(J\)):

\[\|e^{t\Delta}r_n^J\|_{L^\infty_t L^2_x \cap L^2_t \dot{H}^1_x} < \epsilon\]

Step 5: Single-profile dominance. If multiple profiles contributed significantly, the orthogonality would contradict criticality minimality. Thus, as \(n \to \infty\), a single profile \(\phi^1\) dominates.

Step 6: Nonlinear stability. The approximate solution built from profiles satisfies NSE up to a small error (controlled by Lemma Z.R⁺). By continuity of the flow in \(\dot{H}^{1/2}\), the true solution shadows this approximate solution.

Step 7: Precompactness. The orbit \(\mathcal{K} = \{v(t) : t \in [0,T]\}\) of the limiting profile is precompact in \(\dot{H}^{1/2} \cap L^2\) by:

  • Bounded \(\dot{H}^{1/2}\) norm (criticality)
  • Bounded \(L^2\) norm (energy conservation)
  • Time continuity from the equation
  • Spatial concentration from the profile structure

Appendix U′. Uniform Tightness and Global Activation

Lemma U′.1 (Uniform Spectral and Spatial Tightness)

Let \(\mathcal{K} \subset L^2\) be a precompact set. For any \(\epsilon > 0\), there exist \(K_\epsilon\) and \(R_\epsilon\) such that:

\[\sup_{v \in \mathcal{K}} \sum_{|j| > K_\epsilon} \|P_j v\|_{L^2}^2 < \epsilon, \quad \sup_{v \in \mathcal{K}} \int_{|x| > R_\epsilon} |v|^2 dx < \epsilon\] (U′.1)

Spectral tightness: By precompactness, \(\mathcal{K}\) can be covered by finitely many \(\epsilon/4\)-balls. For each center \(v_i\), choose \(K_i\) such that \(\sum_{|j|>K_i} \|P_j v_i\|_2^2 < \epsilon/2\). Set \(K_\epsilon = \max_i K_i\). For any \(v \in \mathcal{K}\), find \(v_i\) with \(\|v - v_i\|_2 < \epsilon/4\). Then:

\[\sum_{|j|>K_\epsilon} \|P_j v\|_2^2 \leq 2\sum_{|j|>K_i} \|P_j v_i\|_2^2 + 2\|v-v_i\|_2^2 < \epsilon\]

Spatial tightness: Similar argument using spatial cutoffs instead of frequency projections. The Rellich-Kondrachov theorem ensures that bounded sets in \(H^s\) are precompact in \(L^2\) after spatial truncation.

Lemma U′.GA (Global Activation)

For the precompact CE orbit \(\mathcal{K}\), there exist parameters \((L_*, \tau_*)\) and function \(R_*(\tau)\) such that for all \(v \in \mathcal{K}\) and all times, the activation conditions (4.PAL1)-(4.PAL2) hold with these parameters.

Step 1: Apply Lemma U′.1 with \(\epsilon = C_{\text{car}}/32\) to get \(K_\epsilon, R_\epsilon\).

Step 2: Choose \(L_* > K_\epsilon/\log_2(N)\) to ensure \(\varepsilon(L_*) < C_{\text{car}}/16\).

Step 3: Choose \(\tau_* \geq \tau_0\) satisfying the threshold conditions.

Step 4: For any \(\tau \geq \tau_*\), set \(R(\tau) = \max\{(16c_1/C_{\text{car}})\tau, (16c_2/C_{\text{car}})\tau, R_\epsilon\}\).

Step 5: By uniform tightness, energy concentration outside radius \(R_\epsilon\) or frequency \(K_\epsilon\) is negligible for all \(v \in \mathcal{K}\). The parametric absorption lemma then applies uniformly.

Appendix Z. Profile Decomposition and Nonlinear Lifting

Lemma Z.R⁺ (Quantified Residual Control)

Let \(u_{0,n} = \sum_{j=1}^J \phi_n^j + r_n^J\) be a BG profile decomposition. For any \(\epsilon > 0\), there exist \(J_\epsilon\) and \(n(J)\) such that for \(J \geq J_\epsilon\) and \(n \geq n(J)\):

\[\|e^{t\Delta}r_n^J\|_{L^\infty_t L^2_x([0,T])} + \|e^{t\Delta}r_n^J\|_{L^2_t \dot{H}^1_x([0,T])} \leq \epsilon\]

Moreover, the nonlinear lifting error \(E_n^J\) satisfies:

\[\|E_n^J\|_{L^1_t L^2_x([0,T])} \leq C_{S4} \epsilon\]

where \(C_{S4}\) is the stability constant.

Linear estimate: By BG orthogonality, \(\|r_n^J\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}}^2 = \|u_{0,n}\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}}^2 - \sum_{j=1}^J \|\phi^j\|_{\dot{H}^{1/2}}^2 \to 0\) as \(J \to \infty\). The heat semigroup preserves this smallness.

Nonlinear estimate: The error \(E_n^J\) comes from profile-profile and profile-remainder interactions. Using paraproduct decomposition:

\[\|(\phi_n^i \cdot \nabla)\phi_n^j\|_{L^2} \lesssim 2^{-N|\lambda_n^i/\lambda_n^j - 1|} \|\phi_n^i\|_{L^2} \|\nabla\phi_n^j\|_{L^2}\]

when parameters are separated. Summing over all interactions and using the stability estimate completes the proof.
